Human artificer & tinkerer

Kree
Aarakocra rogue
Bright-feathered and helplessly drawn to coin. Snatches purses, ‘reads’ books upside down, and keeps a whispering black blade locked in a drawer.

Water genasi, Druid of the Circle of the Sea

Kenra
Kenku, Druid of the Circle of the Moon
Chaos in borrowed voices. Wild-shapes into direwolf, cat, and worse, hoards worthless ‘treasures,’ and carries a past in the northern mountains she’s only beginning to face.

Chapter One

The Road’s Last Teeth

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

The rain does not end all at once.

It fades—slowly, reluctantly—like a breath the sky forgot it was holding.

The wagon creaks along the muddy road, its wheels carving shallow grooves through damp earth. A thin mist lingers in the air, clinging to cloaks and feathers alike. The forest has thinned now, giving way to rolling hills, soft and green beneath a gray sky that is finally beginning to clear.

For the first time since the ravine, since the troll, since the manor—

They rest.

Wilhelm lies sprawled awkwardly against a sack, coat still faintly scorched and damp from earlier misfortunes. One hand rests protectively near his flintlock, even in sleep, his fingers twitching every so often as if reloading in dreams.

Kree lounges atop the wagon’s edge, wings half-draped, idly flipping a coin through his talons. The soft clink, catch, clink is the only sound he contributes to the quiet.

Kenra is upside down. No one knows when that happened. Her legs hang over the side of the cart, head tilted back, beak slightly open as she hums to herself in three different borrowed tones at once.

Thalasstheos sits upright among the ashlings, serene even in exhaustion. The tiny creatures cluster near her warmth, their faint glow flickering like distant lanterns. Her eyes remain half-open, watching the road ahead, even as the drizzle fades completely into stillness.

Peace, for once, seems possible.

Then—

Movement.

Two figures crest a distant rise in the road. One leads a horse; the other walks beside him. Their shapes grow clearer as they approach—travelers, perhaps.

Or something else.

Kree’s coin stops mid-air.

Wilhelm’s eyes open.

The wagon slows.

One of the men steps forward, raising a hand. “Hold there,” he calls, voice rough but measured. His eyes flick over the party, sharp, assessing. “What’ve you got in the cart?”

Wilhelm is already moving.

His hand snaps to his flintlock—

—and stops.

Thalasstheos’s hand rests gently, but firmly, on his wrist.

“No,” she says softly.

The moment stretches.

Kenra leans forward, head tilting sharply, voice bright and suspicious all at once.

“…Are you robbing us?” she asks.

There is a pause.

A long one.

Kree grins.

“Oh, nothing important,” he says breezily, spreading his wings just slightly. “Just a wagon full of—”

He taps the side of the cart.

“—cow manure.”

Silence.

The second man, still a few paces back, snorts—and then scowls.

“Just kill them,” he barks. “Take whatever they’ve got.”

The air snaps.

Kree moves first.

A flash of steel—his dagger whistles through the air—

—and misses.

It thuds harmlessly into the dirt beside the man’s boot.

“Ah,” Kree mutters. “Warming up.”

The road erupts into motion.

The second man charges forward, blade raised, boots splashing through mud as he swings toward Kenra—

—but she is already gone.

She leaps from the wagon—

Midair, her form twists, stretches—

Feathers ripple into fur.

Bones shift with a crack of primal magic.

She lands as a direwolf, massive and snarling, claws digging into the wet earth as she lunges forward with a thunderous pounce.

Jaws snap—

The man stumbles back with a shout, barely avoiding the bite. Panic overtakes him. He scrambles, half-falling, grabbing for the reins of the horse.

❖“NOPE—NOPE—NOPE—”❖

He hauls himself onto the saddle and kicks hard.

The horse bolts.

Gone in seconds.

Kenra skids to a halt, ears flicking, watching him disappear with what might be disappointment.

“Coward,” she growls—though it comes out more like an offended bark.

The remaining man barely has time to react.

He draws his blade—

A sharp crack splits the air.

Wilhelm fires.

The bullet strikes true—not flesh, but steel—blasting the weapon clean from the man’s hand. It spins away into the mud with a dull thunk.

Before he can even curse—

Water answers.

Thalasstheos steps forward, one fluid motion, her arm sweeping outward as if pulling the tide itself from the air. A sudden burst of force slams into the bandit, lifting him off his feet and hurling him backward.

He hits the ground hard.

Does not get up.

Silence falls just as quickly as it came.

The wagon creaks.

The hills breathe.

Kree retrieves his dagger, glancing toward the road where the rider vanished. “Well,” he says lightly, “that could’ve gone worse.”

Wilhelm lowers his flintlock, exhaling slowly. “It almost did.”

Kenra trots back toward the cart, shifting mid-step, fur collapsing back into feathers as she resumes her usual form. “He ran,” she says, sounding mildly offended. “I was winning.”

Thalasstheos says nothing. She simply watches the horizon for a moment longer, ensuring no further threats follow.

Then, quietly—

“Let us keep moving.”

They do.

The road rises gently now, winding between soft hills touched by the last light of a breaking sky. The air smells cleaner here. Less of ash. More of earth.

And then—

A sign.

Weathered wood, half-leaning, but still standing.

❖PHANDALIN❖

Wilhelm exhales.

Kree tilts his head, eyes bright.

Kenra leans forward eagerly.

Thalasstheos watches the town beyond with quiet calm.

At last—

They arrive at the gates.

The road behind them fades into memory, but something tells them—

The journey is only just beginning.

Chapter Two

A Town of Quiet Threads

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

The gates of Phandalin stand open.

Not grand, not guarded by towering stone or iron—but sturdy, worn, and lived-in. The kind of gates that have seen more wagons than wars.

As the party approaches, a guard steps forward, raising a hand in greeting rather than warning.

“Hold there,” he calls, not unkindly. “State your business.”

Kree straightens atop the wagon, already preparing something clever.

“We’ve got a shipment for—” he begins confidently.

He pauses.

A long pause.

Wilhelm sighs.

“It’s for Marta Brin,” he says, stepping forward slightly.

The guard nods immediately, tension easing. “Right. You’re expected.” He steps aside, waving them through. “Welcome to Phandalin.”

And just like that—

They enter.

Phandalin is… quiet.

Not empty. Not lifeless. Just calm.

People move through their day with a simple purpose—carrying goods, sweeping steps, exchanging small words that drift like dust in the sunlight. The kind of place where nothing should go wrong.

They pass a general store, its windows cluttered with tools and travel gear. A tavern hums faintly with midday voices. An inn stands nearby, its sign creaking gently in the breeze.

Normal.

Almost suspiciously so.

The wagon rolls on, wheels clattering softly against packed dirt, until at last they arrive at a more structured building—cleaner, sharper in its edges.

The Merchant’s Guild.

Inside, the air smells faintly of parchment and ink.

Behind a polished desk, a receptionist looks up—then immediately past them, out the window.

At the cart.

At the time of day.

Her expression tightens.

“You’re late,” she says flatly.

Thalasstheos steps forward, calm as ever. “We were… delayed,” she replies gently. “There was a situation.”

A pause.

A very deliberate pause.

The receptionist’s eyes move from her—to Wilhelm—to Kree—

Then stop.

“…I was expecting two,” she says slowly. “A tall young man and a red parrot.”

Kree raises a hand. “Present.”

Wilhelm gestures vaguely. “Yes, well—circumstances changed. They joined us along the way.”

The receptionist’s gaze lingers on Kenra. Then on Thalasstheos.

Judging. Measuring.

Then—she sighs.

“Fine.”

She reaches beneath the desk and produces a coin pouch, placing it down with a soft, definitive thud.

“Payment for delivery.”

Kree moves faster than thought.

The pouch vanishes into his claws.

“Pleasure doing business,” he chirps brightly.

The receptionist does not respond.

Back outside, the town feels warmer somehow.

Less like a checkpoint. More like a place.

And just as quickly as they arrived—

They begin to drift apart.

Kenra wanders first.

Of course she does.

Her steps turn into a curious waddle as she follows something unseen—perhaps a feeling, perhaps nothing at all—until she stumbles upon a small shrine tucked between buildings.

Candles flicker softly.

Charms hang from thin strings.

And standing beside it—

A woman.

Red hair, bright even in the soft light. A pointed hat tilted just slightly. Her presence feels… out of place.

Or perhaps exactly where it should be.

She smiles.

“Welcome,” she says.

Kenra tilts her head. “Fortune?” she asks immediately, producing a few coins with surprising efficiency.

The woman—Ember—accepts them without hesitation.

She studies Kenra.

Not her face.

Her.

Then she speaks.

“You came from the north,” Ember says softly. “From the mountains. A flock of your own kind.”

Kenra stills.

“You were cast out,” Ember continues, voice like quiet flame. “And fell… until you found new companions to carry you forward.”

Silence.

Kenra blinks.

“…Yes,” she says, quieter now.

Ember smiles faintly. From somewhere unseen, she produces a small slip of paper and presses it into Kenra’s hand.

“You will be met with reunion,” she says.

No explanation.

No elaboration.

Just certainty.

Elsewhere—

Wilhelm and Thalasstheos walk side by side.

Kree—

Was with them.

Was.

“…Where is Kenra?” Thalasstheos asks, glancing around.

Wilhelm turns. Pauses.

“…She was just here.”

They both turn the other direction.

“…Where is Kree?” Wilhelm adds.

Silence.

A long, mutual realization settles between them.

They sigh.

Together.

Moments later, they sit on a nearby bench.

Thinking.

Or trying to.

Meanwhile—

Kree has found a door.

Which means, naturally—

He is inside.

The building is tall, quiet, lined with shelves upon shelves of books. The air hums faintly with something arcane.

A mage’s library.

Kree’s eyes gleam.

“Oh… shiny knowledge…” he murmurs, already wandering deeper inside.

“Looking for something?”

He freezes.

Turns.

Ember stands there.

As if she had always been there.

As if distance means nothing at all.

Kree blinks. “…You do that often?”

She only smiles.

“You’re looking for your feathered friend,” she says, gesturing lightly. “The shrine of luck.”

Kree nods slowly… then immediately gets distracted by a shelf.

Books. Old ones. Interesting ones.

Ember watches him for a moment before pulling a worn volume from the shelf and handing it to him.

“For you,” she says.

Kree takes it reverently. “I like her,” he mutters.

Then, with book in hand, he heads back out.

Kenra, meanwhile, has found—

A horse.

More specifically, a stable.

More specifically, a situation.

A woman stands inside the fenced area, instructing a younger rider. Calm. Focused.

Kenra hops the fence.

Immediately.

“Hey—!” the older woman shouts.

Kenra ignores her completely, waddling straight toward the horse with intense curiosity.

“…Hello,” she says, casting a quick spell.

The world shifts.

The horse’s thoughts become words.

The younger rider stares, utterly confused.

Kenra leans in. “Speak,” she demands.

The horse begins—

—but never finishes.

The older woman storms forward, grabs Kenra—

—and throws her.

❖“OUT!”❖

Kenra flies.

Midair—

She shifts.

Feathers collapse into fur as she becomes a cat, twisting gracefully—

Landing on the horse’s back.

“Now we talk—” she begins.

The horse disagrees.

Violently.

It rears, bucks, and launches her with spectacular force—

Across the yard—

Over the fence—

Through the air—

And directly—

At Kree’s feet.

Poof.

Kenra reappears in her normal form, sprawled on the ground.

Kree looks down at her.

Tilts his head.

“…Found you,” he says.

Kenra groans.

Somewhere nearby, Wilhelm rubs his temples without even knowing why.

And Thalasstheos, seated quietly on the bench, exhales as if the tides themselves have decided—

This is simply how things are now.

Phandalin hums gently around them.

Unaware.

Or perhaps—

Waiting.

Chapter Three

The Shape of Something More

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

Kree flips the book open as they walk.

Page after page.

Symbols. Lines. Careful ink.

He squints.

Tilts his head.

Turn it upside down.

“…Huh.”

A long moment passes.

Then another.

Finally, with complete confidence, he snaps the book shut.

“It says,” he declares proudly, “curiosity kills the cat.”

Kenra caws loudly, delighted. “Good book!”

They continue down the road together, two birds with absolutely no intention of questioning this conclusion.

Elsewhere, Wilhelm and Thalasstheos stand near the stables.

The older woman—arms crossed, posture firm—eyes them both with the patience of someone who has dealt with far worse than adventurers.

“Name’s Carmine,” she says. “And yes, I saw your friend.”

Wilhelm sighs. “Which one?”

“The annoying one,” Carmine replies instantly. “Feathers. Noise. Questions.”

“…That narrows it down to both of them,” Wilhelm mutters.

Carmine jerks a thumb toward the street. “I threw her that way.”

Thalasstheos nods politely. “Thank you.”

Wilhelm rubs his temples again.

Kree and Kenra, meanwhile, have discovered a new door.

And once again—

They go inside.

The general store is quiet.

Empty, at first glance.

Crates of furs line the walls. Tools are stacked neatly in rows. Sacks of grain sit beside fresh produce, and coils of rope hang like patient serpents from wooden pegs.

A place of use.

A place of need.

Kree’s eyes immediately lock onto something important.

A bell.

Small. Shiny. Perfect.

He picks it up.

Ring.

A soft voice calls from somewhere behind the counter.

“Coming!”

A moment later, a young woman steps into view—white hair, simple clothes, kind eyes that brighten with surprise.

“Oh—! I wasn’t expecting customers,” she says warmly. “Welcome to Barthen’s Provisions.”

Her gaze lifts to Kree.

“…Your feathers are beautiful,” she adds, genuinely.

Kree puffs up slightly. “I know.”

She leans forward slightly—

“…Is there someone else with you?”

Kenra stands below the counter, completely invisible from this angle.

Kree doesn’t miss a beat. “Yes.”

Kenra pops up just enough to be seen.

Barthen blinks.

Then smiles.

Kree immediately slams the bell on the counter. “I want this.”

Barthen looks at the bell.

Then at Kree.

“…You want the bell.”

“Yes.”

There is a pause.

A long one.

Then, slowly, she nods. “Alright… What are you offering?”

Kree produces the salamander scale with a flourish.

Barthen’s eyebrows lift slightly.

“…Deal.”

The exchange is made.

Kree now owns a bell.

This is a good day.

Kenra, meanwhile, is exploring.

Which means—

She is pecking at the carpet.

Barthen watches her with quiet amusement, saying nothing, as if this is not even remotely the strangest thing she has seen this week.

The birds turn to leave—

The door opens.

Wilhelm and Thalasstheos step inside.

They pause.

See the birds.

See the bell.

Wilhelm exhales.

“…Of course.”

He steps forward and purchases a loaf of bread, tearing into it almost immediately. It is soft. Fresh.

A far cry from ash-covered scraps in a ruined manor.

For a moment, he says nothing.

Just eats.

Thalasstheos offers Barthen a small, grateful nod. “Thank you.”

Barthen smiles gently. “Safe travels.”

They step back out into the town.

And drift, together this time, toward the square.

The fountain sits at its center, water trickling softly.

Kree immediately leans over and drinks from it.

Two nearby townsfolk watch in silent horror—

Then leave.

Quickly.

Kree looks up, water dripping from his beak. “…What?”

Nearby, Thalasstheos stands before a statue.

The stone worn smooth with time.

The founder of the town.

She studies it quietly, something thoughtful in her gaze.

Something… hopeful.

Kree, meanwhile, has gathered everything.

Coin. Spoils. Payment.

He lays it all out.

“Alright!” he chirps. “Time to divide!”

They split the rewards evenly.

No tricks. No sleight of hand.

Just… fairness.

For once.

They each take their share.

And for a brief, shining moment—

They cheer.

A job done.

A road survived.

A victory, small but real.

Then—

The silence returns.

Not heavy.

Not painful.

Just… uncertain.

Wilhelm clears his throat. “Well. I suppose I should return to Brindlewick. The academy will expect me back.”

Kenra shifts. “I… have things to find,” she says vaguely.

Kree shrugs. “Road’s always open.”

Thalasstheos folds her hands gently. “There is still much of the world that needs healing.”

They sit.

Awkwardly.

Four people who were, until recently, strangers.

Now—

Something else.

Something undefined.

Kree’s gaze drifts.

Across the square.

To a building.

Larger than the others.

Marked.

And before it—

A statue.

Bronze.

Four figures stand together, weapons raised, immortalized in triumph.

A party.

A story.

A legacy.

Kree stares at it for a long moment.

Then—

He turns.

“We could do that.”

They look at him.

He gestures toward the statue.

“Not the statue part,” he clarifies. “The adventuring part.”

A pause.

Longer this time.

Real.

“We already fight together,” he continues. “We already travel together. Might as well make it official.”

Kenra tilts her head. “…Group?”

Thalasstheos smiles, something warm and certain. “A shared purpose,” she says. “I would like that.”

Wilhelm sighs.

Deeply.

“…This is a terrible idea,” he mutters.

Then, after a moment—

“…But it is a practical one.”

He looks at them.

Really looks, this time.

“…Fine.”

Kree grins.

Kenra nods eagerly.

Thalasstheos inclines her head.

And just like that—

It is decided.

They stand.

Together.

And begin walking.

Toward the Adventurer’s Guild Hall.

Toward something new.

Not just travelers.

Not just survivors.

But a party.

A beginning.

The road ahead stretches far beyond Phandalin—

Into storm, into mountain—

And into whatever waits beyond.

Chapter Four

Ink, Coin, and a Name Yet Chosen

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

The doors to the Guild Hall swing open with a solid, practiced weight.

Inside—life.

Not the quiet rhythm of Phandalin’s streets, but something louder. Sharper.

Maps stretch across long tables, weighed down by daggers and mugs. Groups of adventurers argue over routes, tracing paths through forests and mountains with ink-stained fingers. A job board clutters one wall—parchments layered atop one another, requests for help, danger, opportunity.

Laughter rises from one corner.

A cheer from another.

Everywhere—experience.

Steel worn from use. Cloaks marked by travel. Eyes that have seen things and lived.

And then—

There are them.

Four figures, damp from the road, slightly singed, slightly disorganized—

Standing in the doorway.

At the front desk, a woman looks up.

Glasses perched low. Expression flat.

She sighs.

“What is it?” she asks, already sounding tired of the answer.

Thalasstheos steps forward, bright and certain. “We would like to register as an adventuring party.”

Kree nods enthusiastically beside her. “Yes. Officially.”

The woman raises an eyebrow.

Her gaze sweeps over them.

“A human. A kenku. A parrot. And a—”

“Water genasi,” Thalasstheos supplies calmly.

“…Right.”

The woman turns without another word, retrieves a clipboard, and places it on the counter with a soft tap.

“Fill it out. Sign at the bottom.”

Her handwriting is neat.

Painfully neat.

Wilhelm leans in immediately, scanning every line with careful attention.

The others—

Do not.

Kenra tilts the page sideways.

Kree flips it upside down.

Thalasstheos waits patiently.

The woman sighs again. “Name’s Casey,” she adds absently.

Wilhelm nods without looking up. “Understood.”

Minutes pass.

Wilhelm reads everything.

Every clause.

Every condition.

Every expectation.

“…Hm,” he mutters.

Kree drums his claws impatiently. “Is it a contract or a novel?”

“Both,” Wilhelm replies.

Eventually—

They sign.

Casey collects the clipboard and, with practiced efficiency, hands each of them a small blue slip of paper.

Blank.

Then—

She produces a single golden slip.

And hands it to Thalasstheos.

The genasi pauses.

Recognition flickers in her eyes.

“A Tablet of Vigor,” she says softly.

Casey nods. “One per party. Annual. Use it wisely.”

A quiet weight settles over the moment.

Life.

Second chances.

Not something to waste.

“Membership fee,” Casey adds flatly.

Coins exchange hands.

The deal is done.

Kree immediately scratches onto his slip.

Carefully.

Deliberately.

“kre”

The k is backwards.

Wilhelm leans over, squinting.

“…Kree,” he says slowly, “that’s for the party name.”

Kree looks up. “Yes.”

“That’s your name.”

Kree nods. “It’s a good one.”

“It is not a party name.”

“It could be.”

Wilhelm opens his mouth—

Thalasstheos steps between them.

“Perhaps,” she says gently, “we should choose something… more meaningful.”

Kree considers this.

Wilhelm exhales.

Kenra nods enthusiastically despite not contributing anything.

Behind the counter, Casey groans.

She takes the forms, stamps them with a dull thunk, and slides them aside.

“Pending,” she mutters.

She gestures vaguely toward the hall. “You’re registered. You can take jobs. Get paid. Don’t die.”

A pause.

“And do at least one job a month,” she adds. “Or you’re disbanded.”

Simple.

Direct.

Final.

They step back out into the evening.

Phandalin has changed.

Not in structure—but in feeling.

Lanterns glow softly now, casting warm halos of orange light along the streets. The air is calm, touched by a gentle wind that carries the faint scent of something magical—something intentional.

The work of a mage, perhaps.

Or simply a town that refuses to let darkness settle too deeply.

The day fades.

The future waits.

They make their way to the inn.

The door opens to warmth.

Voices.

The comforting chaos of food, drink, and people who have chosen, for a moment, to rest.

They sit.

Together.

A round of drinks arrives.

Plates of food follow.

Real food.

Hot.

Fresh.

They eat.

They laugh.

They raise their cups—

“To treasure,” Kree declares.

“To survival,” Wilhelm adds.

“To friendship,” Thalasstheos says softly.

“To… more?” Kenra offers.

The cups meet.

A quiet celebration.

Not loud.

Not grand.

But real.

Later, they approach the innkeeper.

A tall, broad-shouldered tabaxi stands behind the counter, fur dark and sleek, eyes sharp but not unkind.

“Rooms?” he asks.

Wilhelm nods. “Four.”

The tabaxi—Frun—grunts approvingly and produces four keys, placing them on the counter one by one.

“Upstairs,” he says. “Sleep well.”

They climb.

The rooms are simple.

Beds. Tables. Windows.

Enough.

More than enough.

One by one, they step inside.

The doors close.

The town quiets.

And for the first time since the road began—

They rest.

Outside, Phandalin glows softly beneath the night.

The wind moves gently through its streets.

And somewhere, unseen—

Something watches the new party with quiet interest.

The road ahead has begun.

And this time—

They will walk it together.

Chapter Four and a Half

Whispers in the Quiet

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

Night settles gently over Phandalin.

The inn creaks softly, wood shifting as if exhaling after a long day. Outside, lantern light flickers against the windows, casting slow-moving shadows across the walls.

Inside—

Stillness.

For the first time, each of them is alone.

Kenra sits cross-legged on her bed.

A candle burns beside her.

She pokes it.

Once.

Twice.

A third time, slightly harder.

The flame flickers in protest.

Kenra leans closer, fascinated.

“…Fire,” she mutters, as if rediscovering it.

There is nothing urgent in her mind.

No great thought.

No heavy purpose.

Just—

Boredom.

Across the hall, Thalasstheos sits near her window.

A butterfly has found its way inside, wings soft and pale in the dim light. It flutters clumsily, brushing against the glass.

She lifts a hand.

The creature settles gently on her finger.

Nearby, a single ashling lingers—one that did not return with the others. Its faint glow pulses softly, like a quiet heartbeat.

Thalasstheos watches them both.

Small lives.

Fragile.

Free.

A soft smile crosses her face.

For now—

She is content.

Wilhelm sits at a desk.

Candlelight illuminates parchment beneath his hand.

His writing is precise. Careful.

Deliberate.

Each word chosen, weighed.

To the Academy…

The letter continues.

Formal. Respectful.

Final.

He pauses once, pen hovering above the page.

Then continues.

There is no hesitation now.

Only resolve.

When he finishes, he sets the pen down and exhales.

Satisfied.

Kree sits on the edge of his bed.

Coins spill across the blanket in uneven piles.

He counts them once.

Twice.

Then again, just to be sure.

“…Perfect,” he murmurs.

A small, pleased trill escapes him.

Then—

Something shifts.

A sound.

Not from the door.

Not from the hall.

From his bag.

Kree freezes.

“…Hello?”

Silence.

Then—

A voice.

Low.

Cold.

Not spoken aloud—but heard all the same.

“Kree…”

He flinches.

“Kree… attune…”

His claws tremble as he slowly reaches into the bag.

Pulls it out.

The black sword.

The one from the manor.

Its surface drinks the light around it, edges too sharp, too still.

“I am your strength,” the voice murmurs. “Your power… and your vengeance…”

Kree stares at it.

Heart racing.

“I grant you anything…”

A pause.

Long.

Heavy.

“…Attune.”

The room feels smaller.

The air thinner.

Kree’s grip tightens—

Then—

He shoves the blade back into its sheath.

The voice cuts off instantly.

Silence crashes back in.

Breathing hard, Kree scrambles to his bedside drawer, shoves the weapon inside, and slams it shut.

He stares at it.

Doesn’t move.

Doesn’t blink.

“…No,” he whispers.

Kenra’s eyes light up. She is on it in a flash, dagger out, humming.

Kree crouches over a second body. Head cocked one way. Then the other. Squinting with his good eye, brow furrowed in deep scholarly concentration. He straightens, and reports to the group with grave authority:

“…It’s dead.”

A beat. Then Wilhelm snorts — an actual, surprised laugh. “Thank you. Invaluable.”

“I’m only telling you what I see.”

“You’re a credit to the sciences.”

Kenra works briskly down the line of carcasses, prying loose fang after fang, peeling hide free with little satisfied caws. “Good fur,” she declares, packing it away. “Sells. Or hat. Maybe both.”

Then they shoulder their packs, lift their eyes to the cloud-crowned peak above — and start to climb.

✦On the Trail Behind✦

When she finally surfaces — woman-shaped, on hands and knees in the wet grass — the wound is waiting, exactly where she left it. Deep, ragged, four punctures and a tear, the blood already soaking dark through the side of her robe.

She presses a hand flat to it. Her vision sheets white. She breathes the way she has taught a hundred frightened people to breathe — in for four, hold, out for four — until the world settles back into place. “The tide draws back before it returns,” she says, to no one. “Breathe.”

She will not spend a healing spell on herself — not when the others may need every drop she has. But a single small spell is a different arithmetic.

She cups her hands and murmurs the words of Goodberry, and a cluster of pale, glowing berries blooms in her palm — ten little mercies she can ration, rather than one great healing spent all at once and gone. She eats two; the bright magic of them takes the worst of the fire out of the wound.

The rest she does the slow way — the way Doctor Henning showed her, between patients, in the cramped little clinic by the shrine. Boiled cloth and a steady hand, he’d grumbled, and don’t go wasting the fancy stuff on what time and care can close. So she rinses the bite with her waterskin, packs a crushed berry into the punctures, and binds it tight with a strip torn from her hem — the knot pulled with a flat little grunt of pain.

“There,” she says. “You have closed worse than this, with less.” It is not nothing. But saying so helps.

Only then does she look around her — and goes cold in a way the wound never managed. They are everywhere. Displacer beasts, skinned and gutted, left in the brush like discarded coats: hides peeled clean, fangs pried loose. Something is harvesting them.

Her thumb finds the Tele-Communicator. Turns it over once. She thinks of how fast Kree had answered; of how, if her voice shook now, they would turn back — down off that mountain, into this. She will not trade their lives for her own comfort.

She puts the device away. “Not yet,” she says, quiet. “Stay loud. I’m coming.” She drags herself upright, sways, steadies — and walks on, out of the trees, toward the grey sea of reeds.

✦The Climb✦

Higher. The air thinning. Every step a negotiation with gravity.

Kenra hunkers over a fresh kill on the path, picking it apart with a surgeon’s interest. She taps the long, raking gouges in the bone. “These marks,” she says — then mimics, eerily, the deep grinding scrape of something enormous hauling itself over stone. “Not beast. Bigger.”

She lays a palm flat to the body. Her feathers prickle. “Magic. Old. Angry.”

Wilhelm’s face does the thing it does. “I have a bad feeling,” he says.

“You always have a bad feeling,” Kree says cheerfully, already moving.

“And I’m always right.”

“You’re sometimes right.”

“Name one time I was wrong.”

“The bridge troll.”

“…That worked for ten minutes.”

They don’t get far before an injured displacer beast lurches from the rocks at Kree — Wilhelm’s flintlock barks once, clean, and it drops mid-lunge. Its two pack-mates take one look and bolt, scrambling away downslope in open panic.

Wilhelm lowers the gun slowly. “Things that hunt in packs do not run like that,” he says. “Not unless something worse than us is up there.” Nobody answers. They go on anyway.

✦In the Reeds✦

The reeds around Thalasstheos are black now — burnt, snapped, strewn with bones and the bodies of beasts. Her boot nudges something brass: a Tele-Communicator, casing cracked, dead. Her heart drops straight through her.

She lifts her own with trembling hands. “Kree. Kree, answer me—” A crackle. Then his voice, breezy and intact. She has to close her eyes a moment. “…Good,” she breathes. “Good. Stay loud. All of you.”

Then the reeds shift — and a displacer beast looks up from its feeding, blood on its jaws. It lunges. She is already gone, one graceful step through folded space, misty-stepping to its flank. “You’ve fed enough,” she says, almost sad.

The ground answers her. Vines whip up and bind the beast fast, and a shaft of cold silver light falls, and falls, and falls — until it stops thrashing.

Further on she finds another — vast, this one, frozen mid-roar in a glaze of ice she would know anywhere. “Oh, Wilhelm,” she says softly, laying her fingers to the frost. “You were here.” She leaves the dreadful statue to thaw in its own time, and climbs.

✦The Singing Crystal✦

A jut of deep-blue crystal halts the party cold — Kree most of all, his pupils gone wide and shiny. “…Pretty,” he whispers.

Wilhelm snaps a loose shard off the ground and turns it to the light. “Resonant. Conductive, maybe. Interesting—”

Kenra has gone still, head tilted at the stone’s low hum. She reaches out one curious finger. She touches it.

CRACK.

Static jolts up her arm and every feather on her body stands straight out, doubling her size. She sits there — a startled black puffball — blinking.

Then, very deliberately, she reaches over and lays the same finger on Wilhelm. His hair leaps off his scalp in every direction.

Kenra cackles. Kree nearly falls out of his own skin laughing. “…I hate both of you,” Wilhelm says, flattening his hair with both hands. “Worth it,” Kenra says. They move on, still snickering.

✦The Behir✦

The tracks change. Gone are the soft pads of displacer beasts; now there are gouges — deep, dragging furrows scored into the rock, long as a wagon, leading up the trail. Nobody jokes about these.

They follow them to a clearing choked with fallen boulders — and there, at the top of the slope, they see it.

A behir. Long. Serpentine. Wrong — too many legs, a body like a blue-black river of muscle and scale, dragging a displacer-beast corpse in its jaws as easily as a cat carries a mouse.

The party drops as one behind the boulders.

“…Okay,” Wilhelm breathes. “Okay. That. That is the thing.”

“That’s a lot of legs,” Kenra whispers, fascinated.

“Do not count its legs.”

“Twelve.”

“Do not count its legs.”

Kree’s eyes are already up in the branches above them. “I’ll get closer.”

“You will absolutely not—” Wilhelm starts. But Kree is already gone — a soft beat of wings, up into the high boughs of a leaning pine, silent as falling snow. Wilhelm and Kenra press themselves flat into the brush, hardly breathing.

From his perch, Kree watches. The behir hauls its kill to the lip of a dark cave mouth. Drops it. And goes still. Utterly. Wrongly. Still. Its great head lifts. Turns. Tastes the air.

It knows.

“…It stopped,” Kree whispers to no one, forgetting nobody can hear him up there. “Why did it stop? Things shouldn’t stop like that—”

Below, Kenra senses the shift. Quick as instinct, she cups her hands and throws a sound — the skittering chitter of a displacer beast — off to the left, into the rocks, away from them. The behir’s head snaps toward it. Holds.

For one long, breathless second, it works.

Then a branch creaks under Kree’s weight. Just slightly. Just once. The head snaps back — straight up, straight at the tree. Two cold eyes find one half-blind bird.

And the behir roars.

It is not a sound. It is a force — it punches through the chest, rattles teeth, sends pebbles skittering down the slope and birds screaming from the canopy. Kree clamps every feather flat and does not move, does not breathe, is fairly sure his heart simply stops for the duration.

The roar ends. The behir holds his gaze one moment longer — a slow, terrible, measuring moment. Then it turns, takes up its kill, and drags it away into the black throat of the cave.

For a long while, nobody moves. Then Kree drifts down out of the tree, lands on unsteady legs, and the three of them just look at one another.

“In,” Kenra says immediately. “We go in.”

“Have you lost your mind.” It is not a question. “That thing is the length of a barn. It looked at a tree and the tree apologized. We are three people. One of you eats potatoes mid-combat.”

“That was one time, and I was hungry—” Kree protests.

“We are equipped, at most, for a large angry dog.”

“Wilhelm,” Kenra says, patient as a knife. “It dragged food inside. Hoard. Things. Treasure-things.”

Kree’s head comes up. “…Treasure-things?”

“Do not say treasure-things to him.”

But it is already too late. Kree has wandered three steps off and is crouched over a scatter of blue crystal in the dirt, head tilted, the great roaring monster apparently already filed under later. Wilhelm pinches the bridge of his nose.

Kenra, meanwhile, has gone quiet and thoughtful — which is worse. She looks at the cave, then back down the broken slope they climbed. “It makes sense now,” she says, and her borrowed voices drop into something almost her own. “The beasts. Down in the towns. Everyone said they came out of nowhere — got bold, got hungry, started hitting the caravans and the farms.” She nods at the cave. “They didn’t come down for sport. That drove them off the mountain. Hunted them. Ate them. They ran into people because they were running from it.”

“Something’s wrong up here,” she finishes. “Bigger than lizards. Bigger than beasts.”

Wilhelm looks at the cave mouth for a long, long moment. “…I want it on record,” he says finally, “that I think this is a terrible idea.”

“Noted,” says Kenra. “Noted,” says Kree, not looking up from the crystals. “It is now in the record.”

“It’s a very good record,” Kenra agrees, and starts toward the cave. They case the mouth of it — low, careful, hugging the rock — and one by one, they slip inside.

✦The Cold Camp✦

Thalasstheos crests a rise and finds it: their cold campfire, scuffed ground, four sets of footprints heading up. Relief and dread in the same breath. She looks up the long throat of the mountain, sets her hat, and starts to climb — lifting the Telecom to her ear as she goes, meaning only to hear their voices once more.

Chapter Nine

Into the Ravine

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

The tunnel slopes down into warm, breathing dark.

Across a yawning black gap, two small lizards bask in the glow of a blue crystal, fat and lazy. Far below, in the throat of the ravine, more eyes kindle — a dozen, two dozen, cold sparks in the deep.

Kree, of course, simply glides across.

“Kree,” Wilhelm hisses. “Kree, we have not discussed crossing yet—”

“It’s easy. You just have wings.”

“I do not have wings, that’s the entire problem — get back here—”

And then Kree’s pocket buzzes. Time slows.

“…Kree,” Wilhelm says, very quietly. “Do not.”

Kree answers it.

Thalasstheos’s voice spills bright and worried into the cavern — “Kree, are you all—” — and two pairs of basking eyes snap open.

The screech that follows is not a sound so much as a blade — high, splitting, echoing down into the ravine — and the answer rises out of the dark: a chorus of clicks and chitters and the dry scrape of claws on stone, as every cold spark below begins to climb.

“KREE!” Wilhelm’s voice cracks across the gap. “WHY. WHY WOULD YOU ANSWER THE—”

“She sounds upset! Hold on — Thalasstheos? You’re breaking u—”

“PUT IT DOWN—”

A lizard erupts over the lip and launches itself at Kree’s face. He yelps, flails, beats backward — and the Telecom spins from his grip, end over end, glinting once in the crystal-light before the dark below swallows it.

“…Oh,” says Kree. “OH?” says Wilhelm.

And then the ledge is full of teeth.

Wilhelm stops shouting and starts working. He throws himself flat at the edge, props the flintlock, and picks them off as they climb — one shot, a shriek, a body peeling off the wall and dropping into nothing. Reload. Powder, ball, ram, the motions automatic even as his voice is not. “There are too many,” he calls. “Kenra — I need the gap held—”

Kenra grins with too many teeth. “BEHOLD,” she announces, “the consequences of your actions—” And she leaps.

Fur bursts outward, bone stretches and pops, and a wolf the size of a pony clears the chasm in one furious bound, landing snarling on the far ledge beside Kree. A lizard whips around and sinks its teeth into Kree’s arm; he shouts. Another rears back, throat swelling, scales crackling blue with gathering lightning, jaw cracking wide—

Kenra’s jaws close on it first. One savage shake — and it bursts into a fistful of grey ash that drifts down into the dark.

“…Okay,” Kree wheezes, clutching his arm, “the wolf was a good idea.”

“I am full of good ideas,” says the wolf, around a mouthful of lizard.

But they keep coming — up over the lip, more and more — and then onto the walls, and the ceiling, clinging at impossible angles, skittering overhead, raining grit into everyone’s eyes.

“They’re on the roof now,” Wilhelm reports, with the flat despair of a man whose day has run entirely out of road. “Of course they’re on the roof.”

Kree draws the black sword. It drinks the crystal-light, edges too dark — and for once he does not hesitate. He moves, wings flaring, carving a bright arc through the press of them, two lizards unmaking themselves on the blade in a hiss of nothing.

Wilhelm tilts his aim up. Squints. Adjusts for the angle, the dark, the way the whole cavern shivers. “…Everyone hold still,” he mutters, and fires — not at a lizard, but at the root of a great stalactite hanging over the climbing horde.

It cracks. It falls — a spear of stone the length of a man — and takes a whole knot of them down into the ravine with it, in a clatter of rock and a chorus of fading screeches.

“HA,” says Wilhelm, genuinely surprised. “That worked.”

“Don’t sound so shocked,” Kree calls. “I’m always shocked when things work—”

A lizard on the ceiling above Kree drops its jaw, and the air goes white.

The lightning catches Kree full in the chest. It lifts him off his feet and slams him down, every feather standing on end and smoking — and Kenra’s head snaps around, and for one awful second the whole cavern is just the after-image of the bolt and the smell of burnt feathers —

— and then the lizard that fired it, spent and overcharged, simply detonates, and the blast takes the one clinging beside it too, both bursting into twin clouds of ash that sift down through the crystal-glow.

Kree lies on the stone. Smoking. Blinking up at the ceiling. “…Ow,” he says, with feeling.

Then silence rings out, sudden and total. Somewhere, water drips. The last of the dust settles. Two of them and a wolf stand among the ash, chests heaving, the crystal humming its low blue note over a ledge gone quiet at last.

The Telecom — Kenra’s, now — buzzes. She trots over, still half-wolf, noses it up with one paw, and shifts back to feathers as she lifts it.

“Kenra? Kenra, what happened — I heard screaming, I heard lightning—” Thalasstheos’s voice is pulled wire-tight.

“…Lizards,” Kenra reports, surveying the ash. “Many. Dead now.” A pause. “Kree answered his phone.”

“I’m fine,” Kree croaks from the floor. “Mostly.”

A hundred miles of mountain away, Thalasstheos closes her eyes, presses a hand to the binding on her aching side, and climbs a little faster. And worries a great deal more.

Chapter Ten

The Heart of the Mountain

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

The next passage is narrow, and Wilhelm takes the front of it — flintlock low, shoulder to the cold stone, moving with the careful, unhappy precision of a man who has decided that if they must do this, they will at least do it quietly.

“Slow,” he breathes. “Hug the wall. Do not touch anything that—”

Ahead, a soft blue glow pools around the bend. Crystals. A whole vein of them. Kree’s eyes go to coins.

“Kree—” He is already drifting toward it, feathers fluffed, the rest of the world dissolving into a single shining want. He rounds the corner — and stops.

Because the crystals are not unattended. Coiled across the glittering bed of them, vast and patient and faintly luminous, lies the behir. Up close it is somehow worse — the slow bellows of its breathing, the dozen limbs folded under it, the long body draped over the hoard like a king who fell asleep mid-thought.

Kree makes a sound. A small one. A bird one. He scrambles back behind a stalagmite, flattens himself to it, clamps his beak shut, and prays.

Too late.

The great head lifts. The tongue tastes the air, once, twice — and a low voice rolls through the chamber, older than the stone.

“…I smell feathers.” A pause. “And fear. Come out, little carrion-bird.”

✦The Behir’s Bargain✦

Kree edges out from behind the stalagmite, every feather pressed flat, and gives the monster a smile that is roughly nine-tenths terror.

“…Hi. Hello. Big fellow. Lovely cave. Really catches the light—”

“Where are the others.” Not a question. The head lowers until one cold eye is level with him. “You did not climb my mountain alone. The thunder-thrower. The one who wears other skins. Where.”

Kree’s mind, for once in its life, moves fast.

“Gone,” he says. “Long gone. Turned back at the reeds — too scary, they said, far too many legs — and honestly? Smart. Wish I’d gone with them. I just, ah—” he gestures vaguely at the crystals — “got lost. Looking for the way out. Which I will now do. Quietly. Forever.”

The behir studies him for a long, terrible moment. Then it snorts — a gust of ozone and contempt — and lays its head back down.

“You are barely a mouthful,” it rumbles. “Not worth the chewing. Turn back, little bird. Crawl down my mountain the way you came. If I taste you in these halls again—” the eye slides shut — “I will not waste the breath to warn you.”

And it rises, fluid and immense, and pours itself out of the chamber into the dark of the next room, scales whispering over crystal.

Silence. Kree stands very still and discovers he has forgotten how to breathe. From the passage behind him, three shapes detach from the shadow — Wilhelm, Kenra, and the lingering ghost of a wolf — who have, it turns out, watched the entire thing.

“…You,” Wilhelm says slowly, “lied to a behir. To its face.”

“I panicked eloquently,” Kree wheezes.

“It worked.”

“I’m as surprised as you are.”

✦The Outcrop✦

But Kree is already over it. The fear drains out of him the instant the danger leaves the room, and the crystals reclaim his whole attention. He crouches at the hoard, head tilting, beak open in quiet wonder.

Kenra, still wolf-shaped, pads to the great crystal bed where the behir had lain — and there, half-buried in the glittering nest, sits a large, strange potion, its liquid catching the light. Because the cave is dark and a wolf has limited options, she opens her jaws and a curved blade of orange fire blooms into being, clenched between her teeth — a torch with a heartbeat.

The warm orange glow throws everyone’s shadow huge across the walls, and for a moment it is the manor again — that long dark night weeks and a lifetime ago, Kenra padding ahead of them down the halls with the very same firelight in her teeth, lighting the way through someone else’s nightmare. Wilhelm notices it too. Neither of them says so.

She trots the light over, drops the flaming scimitar with a clack, and picks the potion up in her mouth instead.

Across the chamber, Wilhelm has found the other feature of the room: a pit, low in the floor, where a freshly killed displacer beast lies torn open — and a knot of lizards crouches over it, feeding, oblivious, tails twitching in the dark.

Kree, meanwhile, has found The One. A single crystal, larger and clearer than the rest, jutting from the rock at the perfect angle to be his. He wraps both hands around it and pulls. Nothing. Braces a foot. Pulls. Nothing. He thinks. He reaches into his bag, rummages with the focus of a surgeon — and produces a hammer.

Wilhelm turns just in time to see it. “Kree. Kree, do not—”

CRACK.

The crystal breaks free. And so does something else. A wave of cold energy rolls out from the shattered stone — silent, total, washing through the whole chamber like a held breath let go. And everywhere it touches, magic simply… leaves.

The wolf-shape sloughs off Kenra in an instant, the borrowed muscle and fur unraveling until she is just a small kenku where a pony-sized predator had been. The fire on the dropped scimitar gutters, shrinks, and winks out, the blade dissolving into nothing — and the cave goes dark, lit now only by the soft, indifferent glow of the crystals themselves.

Kenra stands in the new gloom, feathers slowly rising. She turns to Kree. She glares. “…You,” she says, in a flat little voice entirely her own, “will not do that again.”

“I felt that in my teeth,” Wilhelm hisses, smacking the side of a device on his belt as its lights stutter and die. “My instruments — the whole frequency just — Kree.”

Kree spreads his hands, the crystal still clutched in one, and adopts an expression of wounded innocence. “Okay, first of all,” he says, “that completely wasn’t my fault.”

✦The Glowing Blue✦

The cold wave does not stop at them. It rolls on — across the floor, into the pit — and the feeding lizards go rigid, heads snapping up off their kill, eyes kindling in the dark.

Wilhelm does not wait for them to think it through. “Move — move, now—” and he has a fistful of Kree’s collar and a fistful of Kenra’s cloak, hauling both into the next passage before the lizards can place where the disturbance came from.

The room beyond is black. No crystals here. No light at all. Kenra, still holding a grudge and a potion, considers the bottle in her hands. She uncorks it. She drinks it.

She begins, gently, to glow — but this is not the fire. This is not the warm orange of the manor halls, of the scimitar in her teeth. This light is cold — a deep, electric blue, rising off her feathers like light through deep water, beautiful and wrong, the exact hue of the crystals and the lightning and everything on this mountain that wants them dead. Wilhelm looks at her in it and does not like how much she matches the place.

“…Useful,” he decides anyway, because useful is what they have. “Right. You’re the lamp. Walk slowly.”

They make it perhaps ten feet before a shape in the dark catches the blue light, turns, and screeches — and the room comes alive with teeth.

✦On the Rocky Path✦

A storm’s worth of miles below, Thalasstheos is not having a gentler night. Three displacer beasts have her circled on the open trail, and the rain has started in earnest. She moves through them like water finds the low ground — patient, economical, refusing to be where they expect her.

The first lunge is a feint; she reads the false image and lets her ice pass through it, into the rock beyond. The second beast she takes clean, a lance of cold silver light folding it into the mud.

The third gets to her. It hits her shoulder and drives her back into a pine, pinning her against the bark, jaws gaping for her throat — and finding, in the half-second before they close, that she has a hand free.

“Not tonight,” she says, almost kindly. A shaft of moonlight punches down through the canopy and through the beast’s torso, and it sags off her, and is still.

She straightens. Touches the gouge on her shoulder, winces, leaves it. The weather is turning — the wind sharpening, the clouds stacking black against the peak. And then she sees it: a great crystal jutting from the mountainside, taller than she is, humming with a low blue light. She slows. Lays her palm against it. Feels the wrongness in it. “…So this is what has been calling them down,” she murmurs. And keeps climbing.

✦The Thunder in the Dark✦

In the black room, the fight is going badly in the way fights go badly when you cannot see. Lizards wink in and out of the blue light — there, then gone, then there again at your flank — and the party fights blind around their one glowing lamp.

Wilhelm fires at shapes and shadows. Kree carves where he can. Kenra, stripped of fire and fur both, has fallen back on the oldest weapon she owns: a quarterstaff, swung with grim, glowing fury, cracking against scaled skulls.

They give ground up a rocky slope, dodging the white stutter of lightning, beating back the ones that pounce — and then something bigger drops down in front of them. A lizard half again the size of the rest, throat already swelling. It hits the ground and a wave of thunder rolls out of it — not through the air but through the stone, a current that finds every boot and bare foot in the room.

The whole party seizes and staggers as it hits them at once. And in that frozen half-second, the big lizard’s jaws come down over Kree and close, swallowing him to the wings.

“KREE—” Wilhelm wheels, fires, nearly takes a lightning bolt across the face for the trouble, jerks back with his ears ringing. Kenra is a thrashing blue smear in the dark, swinging her club at anything that moves, screaming something that might be a war cry and might just be screaming.

✦The Healer Arrives✦

The cave entrance finds Thalasstheos the way an answer finds a question. She rushes in, hat shedding rain — and pulls up hard at the lip of the great ravine, the black gap, a single broken boulder far below.

She does not hesitate. One step into folded space, and she is on the far side, staff lifting in her hand with a soft pearl light kindling at its head.

The next room has feeders in it. They smell her before she clears the passage, and four come off their kill at once. She fights them the way she fights everything — without panic, without waste. Vines burst from the floor to snare the first two; a whip of conjured water slaps a third off its feet and into the wall; the fourth she simply sidesteps, calm as a tide pool, and lets the moonlight take it.

But the commotion in the next room pulls her eye for one fatal heartbeat — voices, familiar voices, screaming — and in that heartbeat the lightning finds her. It throws her down. The lizards pile on, a scrabbling weight of claws and teeth, and for one bad moment she is flat under all of them, an arm up, the light of her staff guttering against the stone—

Then a screech rings out from the far room — high, summoning — and as one the lizards abandon her, scrambling toward the sound. She lies still a moment. Breathes. In for four, hold, out for four. Then she gets up and follows them.

And the room she steps into stops her cold. Kree has fought his way out of the big lizard’s mouth and onto its neck, riding the thrashing thing, driving his black sword down into its skull, over and over, with a single-minded fury she has never once seen in him. Kenra stands over a downed lizard, beating it into the floor with her staff, glowing blue and absolutely feral. Wilhelm is pressed to a wall, reading the room with a tactician’s cold eye, sliding away from every strike that comes for him.

She does not waste the moment. “Down — all of you, down,” she calls, and lifts her hands, and the cool tide of her healing washes through the room and finds each of them at once. “I have you. Hold the line a little longer.”

Three heads turn toward her voice in the gloom. “…Oh thank the gods,” Wilhelm breathes. “The competent one is here.”

✦The Run for the Tunnel✦

The healing buys them their feet back, but not the room. There are simply too many, and more coming, and the big lizard is rising again. It is Kenra who spots it — a narrow tunnel mouth in the far wall, dark and low.

“THERE — go, go, everybody go—”

She bolts first, a blue streak. Thalasstheos comes second, herding Kree ahead of her with a hand at his back. Kree dives in third. And Wilhelm comes last — and last is where the mountain catches him.

A lizard slithers across the tunnel mouth and blocks it, and sinks its teeth into his leg as he tries to pass; he shouts, staggers. And behind him the big lizard comes scuttling fast across the floor, jaws cranking wide, low and hungry and certain.

A hand shoots out of the tunnel. Kree’s. He shoves the blocking lizard bodily aside and yanks — and Wilhelm tumbles through into the dark just as the big lizard’s jaws snap shut on empty air where his leg had been. The thing slams its head against the tunnel mouth, claws raking, too large to follow, screaming its fury into a hole it cannot fit.

Chapter Eleven

No Way Back

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

On the other side, gasping, they look up. And find themselves in a cathedral of crystal.

It is the heart of it. The vein they had been chasing splinters here into a vast humming chamber, every wall and ceiling studded with glowing blue stone. The source. The thing that has been driving the mountain mad. And it is, they realize one by one, a beautiful place to die in.

Wilhelm does the survey first, because he always does, walking the walls with a tin lantern of a face. The tunnel they came through is sealed behind them. The way out is buried under rockfall. The only living door has a behir-sized lizard standing watch on it. He counts the exits twice, the way you check a sum you do not believe, and gets the same answer.

“…We’re in a box,” he says quietly. “A pretty one.”

Nobody argues. That is the worst part. There is no quip from Kree, no theatrics from Kenra — just four people who climbed a mountain to help some farmers and are now cornered in its gut, blood drying on them, the air crackling with a power that eats the only things they are good at. They are not adventurers anymore. They are something smaller and older than that. They are prey, run to ground, waiting for the thing that hunts this peak to remember they are in here.

Thalasstheos moves among them with her hands glowing, closing what she can, and pauses at Kenra — tilting her head at the cold blue light still pouring off her. “…That is new,” she observes mildly.

“Long story,” Kenra says.

“They always are.” Thalasstheos presses a small green vial into her hands. “Drink. It will give you back what the mountain took.”

Then they try to think their way out, and it goes like this.

“We break the crystals,” Kenra says first, hefting her staff. “Kill the source. The lizards go quiet, maybe the big one too—”

“And the blast unmakes you where you stand,” Wilhelm cuts in. “Like Kree’s little hammer trick, except surrounded by it. Her—” he points at Thalasstheos — “and you go down with the stone. No.”

“Then we dig,” Kree offers, eyeing the rockfall. “The buried tunnel. I have a hammer—”

“You have done enough with the hammer.”

“It was a good hammer—”

“It would take a day,” Thalasstheos says gently, and that ends it, because they do not have an hour, let alone a day.

“Fine. The lizard at the door.” Wilhelm rubs his eyes. “We can’t fight through it — not all of us, not hurt like this. The first one through dies.”

“So no one goes through,” Thalasstheos says slowly, and the others look at her. “It is a hunter. A hunter follows the loudest, brightest thing in the dark. If something draws it off the door — just for a breath — the rest of us are gone before it turns back.”

A silence while it sinks in. “…That something gets shot at by lightning,” Wilhelm says. “Yes.” “Alone.” “Yes.”

✦The Distraction✦

Kree, with the instant generosity of a man volunteering someone else, brightens. “Kenra! She glows! She does voices! She’s a perfect—”

“Kree.”

“—she could sound like a whole herd of—”

“Kree. Whose hammer started this?”

A pause. Three faces turn to him with the serene unanimity of a jury. “…Oh, come on,” Kree says.

He sighs the long sigh of the unjustly accused, and flies out the tunnel in a burst of indignant red feathers. He bursts up into the open chamber and grabs the loudest thing he owns — Barthen’s bell — and rings it like the end of the world, swooping through the air, well above the floor. Can’t reach me up here, he thinks, smug, can’t possibly—

The first lightning bolt parts the feathers an inch from his head.

“OKAY — okay, it can do that — HURRY UP — PLEASE HURRY UP—” and Kree becomes a frantic, jinking, screaming smear of red against the blue, dodging bolt after bolt as the big lizard wheels beneath him, thunder-throat blazing.

Below, they run for it. Wilhelm goes first, scrambling up into the climb-tunnel. Thalasstheos next. Then Kenra — but Kenra’s blue glow betrays her the instant she breaks cover, and the big lizard’s head snaps around toward the light. She dives behind a rock just in time.

Kree, aloft, sees it turning away from him and tries everything to win its eye back. It does not bite. Kenra, pinned behind her rock with a glowing monster between her and escape, lets go of every sensible instinct she has and reaches for the one absurd thing left to her. She makes herself very still. She dims. She hunches. She does an impression of a glowing blue rock.

From the tunnel, Wilhelm watches this happen in real time. “…Is that going to work,” he says faintly. “Is she — what’s the sound of a rock. Does she have a rock noise. I need to know if she has a rock noise—”

It works.

The behir-sized lizard sweeps its gaze right over the small motionless blue lump, decides it is scenery, and turns back to fling lightning at the loud red bird. Kenra waddles — waddles, with enormous dignity — to the tunnel, where two pairs of hands haul her up and in.

And Kree, freed at last, folds his wings and dives, loosing one arrow on the way past — not at the lizard, but at a great loose slab above the tunnel mouth. It drops. It seals the passage behind him in a roar of stone, the big lizard’s scream cutting off mid-note.

They are through. There is no longer a way back.

✦Skathrix✦

The passage opens into one last chamber. And the behir is waiting for them.

It is coiled in the center of the room, and it is smiling — if a thing with that many teeth can smile — a slow, sadistic, unhurried grin. It uncoils as they enter and begins to circle, the long body sliding around the edge of the room, the cold eyes traveling up and down each of them in turn. The tongue flickers out, tasting. The tail flicks, testing. It is not afraid. It is choosing.

Kree, because Kree cannot help it, opens his beak to say something. Wilhelm’s hand clamps over it. But it is enough. The eyes fix on them, and the grin drops away.

“You were warned, little bird,” the behir says — and its voice fills the room now, no longer bored, enormous. “I am Skathrix. I have ruled this peak since before your towns had names. And you — you crawled back into my house.”

Its throat begins to swell. The whole chamber crackles blue. “Then choke on the storm.”

The lightning does not strike. It fills — the entire room going white and screaming at once. Wilhelm is hurled off his feet and does not get up. Kree drops where he stands. Thalasstheos crumples last, slow, fighting it the whole way down, and ends on the floor between them with her eyes half-open and the light gone out of her hands.

Only Kenra is left standing.

She does not understand it — the storm rolled through her and past her, the cold blue light still wrapped around her, untouched. (The potion. Of course. Lightning.) But understanding it does not help, because understanding it does not wake the others, and the others are not waking.

She stands frozen in the smoking quiet as Skathrix lowers itself to the floor and begins to pour across the stone toward them — slow, certain, savoring it. This is the part it likes. This is why it did not simply crush them in the dark. It wants to watch the small glowing one understand that there is nothing left to do.

And there isn’t. Kenra knows it even as she drops to her knees among them — shaking Wilhelm, shaking Kree, pressing both hands to Thalasstheos’s chest, hunting for the rise and fall of breath, finding it faint, finding it fading — one small bird in a cathedral of cold light, the only thing still moving, and not nearly enough.

The mountain has them. It was always going to have them. Skathrix slides closer, and opens its mouth, and there is no one left to stop it.

Chapter Twelve

The Debt Collector

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

The notice on the Phandalin job board is short, and the pay is fair.

“The healer Thalasstheos, of the Phandalin clinic, gone to the storm peaks and not returned. Find her. Bring her home. — Q. Alderleaf.”

Lloyd reads it twice, the way he reads everything — slowly, and all the way through. An orc in a patched traveling coat, an old iron spade strapped across his back. Aurys teaches that a debt unpaid only grows, and half of Phandalin owes that healer something. He signs his name in careful block letters, shoulders his pack, and starts walking.

The storm meets him halfway up the mountain. It doesn’t feel like weather. The lightning crawls instead of flashing, and the thunder, if you listen wrong, sounds like breathing. The trail leads him past a feeding pit — bones picked clean and scorched — and into tunnels where the walls glow blue-white, veined with crystal like frozen lightning.

He follows the light down.

Sounds reach him first: stone cracking, a roar that isn’t thunder, and a voice — hoarse, furious, refusing to stop. The tunnel opens into a vast cavern. Four strangers lie broken across the crystal floor. Above them coils a serpent-dragon the size of a barn, scales sparking, jaws wide. And between the beast and the fallen stands one small kenku, sickles raised.

Lloyd steps out of the tunnel mouth. “You look like you could use a hand. Aurys teaches that a debt unpaid only grows — let’s settle this beast’s, and yours.”

✦The Rescue✦

He doesn’t reach for a weapon first. He reaches for the fallen. Cure Wounds, once. Twice. Three times. Golden light pools from his rough green hands, and one by one the strangers gasp, cough, and rise — a rogue in a grey cloak snatching up a black sword, a water-haired woman pushing to her feet with the storm’s light bending in her eyes.

Thalasstheos doesn’t waste breath on thanks. Wilhelm is breathing but done — she gets her shoulder under him and carries him to the tunnel mouth, out of the beast’s reach. Water coils from her wrists as she seals the worst of his wounds and sets his rifle across his lap.

“Rest. You’ve given enough today.” Wilhelm, for once, does not argue. He sits this one out, watching from the tunnel with his rifle on his knees.

✦Skathrix, Unamused✦

Above them, Skathrix Coilborn watches his kills stand back up — and takes it personally. His roar shakes crystal dust from the ceiling, and his whole coiled length comes off the stone at once, fury crawling down the cavern walls with him.

Kenra moves first. She pulls a stoppered vial from her belt and drinks it in one motion — and her wildshape and spellwork come flooding back like a tide returning. Her sickles hit the floor. What charges the behir is a dire wolf, glowing blue as deep ice.

Kree goes the other way — up into the crystal spires, grey cloak flickering between the lightning veins. His bow sings once, twice, three times, arrows hissing into the serpent’s flank while Skathrix’s jaws crash through the stone where the party stood a heartbeat ago.

Thalasstheos plants herself at the fight’s edge and heals — water spiraling out to wherever the fight bleeds. Every wound Skathrix earns, she takes back.

And Lloyd runs. Not away — under. He drops into a slide across the rain-slick crystal, straight beneath the rearing coils, both hands on the old iron spade. Golden light bursts down the haft. The Divine Smite cracks against the serpent’s jaw like a falling bell. Scales shatter. The cavern goes white.

✦The First Breath✦

Skathrix rears back, and the light inside him changes — a charge building behind his ribs, the wall-crystals ringing in sympathy. But he doesn’t spend it yet. First he wants them to hurt.

Talons rake Kree out of the air mid-shot. In the same breath, the serpent’s jaws close on Lloyd, lift him clean off his feet, and slam him back down. Lloyd hits the ground, spits blood — and crawls to Kree first. Lay on Hands. Warm gold spreads from his palm, and the rogue’s wounds simply close.

Thalasstheos, mid-cast, watches her patient get poached. “Oh, that’s how it is.”

Ice condenses out of the wet air — a fan of knives shrieking across the cavern into the behir’s flank — and behind them she sends the water itself, a whip-crack column that hammers the serpent’s shattered jaw.

Skathrix takes it all. Then his jaws unhinge, and the charge he’s been holding lets go — lightning erupts in a blinding line, forking off every crystal in the walls. There is nowhere it doesn’t reach.

When the light dies, every one of them is still standing. Barely.

✦The Bite✦

Skathrix isn’t finished with her. His head lashes past the ice-knives faster than thought, and his fangs close on Thalasstheos — deep, lightning-hot, punching through robe and water-ward alike. Her cry cracks against the cavern walls. The behir’s jaws lock, and he lifts her — then the world turns sideways as he climbs, flowing up the cavern wall like water running in reverse, Thalasstheos clenched in his teeth like a prize. He shakes her, once, hard, for the insolence of those ice knives. Forty feet of storm-lit stone falls away beneath her feet.

Kenra goes up after them. The blue wolf hits the wall at a dead run and simply keeps running — claws finding every seam and crystal ledge — and when she reaches the behir’s trailing length she sinks her fangs in with primal savagery, down to the bone.

Skathrix convulses. His jaws wrench open — and Thalasstheos falls. Forty feet of empty dark. Bleeding. Limp.

A grey blur hits the air beneath her. Kree — wings snapped wide, arrows abandoned — catches her out of the dark and spirals them both down hard, rough and graceless and alive, skidding across the stone in a tangle of cloak and feathers.

Above, the behir coils around the wolf on his flank and squeezes. Kenra vanishes into loops of sparking scale. Below, Lloyd plants his feet, blood in his teeth, and raises his free hand. Command — one word, ringing with borrowed gold:

“Come.”

And Skathrix Coilborn — ancient, furious, and suddenly not entirely his own — releases the wolf and comes for the orc.

✦Introductions, Again✦

The behir does not strike. He draws himself up to his full height, and when he speaks, the cavern speaks with him.

“I am SKATHRIX COILBORN. Warden of the deep tunnels. Fang beneath the Sky Sovereign. And you — you will tell me WHO DARES.” — with the wounded dignity of a creature saying it for the second time and furious about it.

Nobody answers fast enough. The charge comes back into his throat — deeper, meaner — and this time there is no first target. He unleashes it on all of them at once. Lightning fills the cavern wall to wall. No floor, no ceiling, no party. Just the storm, indoors.

And then, out of the heart of the blast, walks Thalasstheos. Soaked. Furious. Upright.

Water spins around her wrists, and she flings it wide — silver threads lashing through the afterglow to find every member of the party where the lightning threw them. Wounds close. Breath returns. One by one, the fallen stir and stand.

“Stay with me.” — quiet, firm, absolute. Then, to the serpent above, calm as deep water: “You don’t get them.”

✦The Counterattack✦

She raises her hand, and the cavern remembers the moon. Moonbeam tears down through the storm-dark in a column of cold silver — and Skathrix, impossibly fast for something the size of a bridge, pours himself out of its path along the ceiling.

Lloyd hefts a javelin and puts his whole shoulder — his whole faith — into the throw. It sails past the behir’s head with tragic majesty and shatters a crystal forty feet up.

“…Aurys saw that. Nobody else needs to.”

But every dodge has a price. Skathrix flows away from the moonbeam, away from the javelin — and directly over Kree. The rogue is already moving. A grey flicker off the crystal spire, wings tucked, the black sword in both talons — and he opens the behir’s flank in one long, silent line. The blade doesn’t spark or ring. It drinks — and where it cuts, the storm-light in Skathrix’s body gutters like a lamp behind a closing door.

For the first time, the Warden of the deep tunnels makes a sound that isn’t fury.

✦The Squeeze✦

Skathrix answers with his tail. It comes around like a ship’s mast swung by a storm, catches Kree square, and swats him across the cavern. Then the behir lets go of the ceiling entirely — twelve tons of serpent pouring down the wall in one liquid rush — and before the dust rises he is around them: Thalasstheos and Lloyd both, two loops apiece, closing like a drawn knot.

He squeezes. Her water bursts into mist. Lloyd’s spade arm is pinned, gold light stuttering between the coils. The furnace eyes come down level with theirs, and the pressure climbs — slow, deliberate, savoring. Both healers, wrapped in the same fist.

Kree’s talons find what’s left of his rope. No plan worth explaining — just a shout: “KENRA!”

The rope spins through the storm-light. The wolf understands. She catches it out of the air and runs — a blue streak circling the behir’s bulk, once, twice, threading the line around that great sparking neck and pulling it singing-tight.

Inside the coils, Lloyd finds three inches of room and spends them on someone else — shoving Thalasstheos free of the loops and taking a fresh crush of scale across his chest for it. She hits the ground rolling, comes up on her knees, and slams both palms to the stone. Entangle.

“Grow.”

Roots and pale vines erupt from every crack, lashing the behir’s coils to the floor he’s owned all his life. The rope goes taut as Kenra hauls his horned head down, four paws braced, pinning it against the stone. And Lloyd — half-crushed and absolutely unbothered — tears himself free of the loosening coils.

The Warden of the deep tunnels lies bound. Vines below. Rope above. Wolf-weight on his skull.

✦The Guilt Trip✦

— Fun table moment, mostly canon, definitely happened —

Lloyd, freshly torn free, does not press the attack. He limps up to the serpent’s enormous pinned face, leans on his spade like a farmer surveying a disappointing field, and starts making him feel bad about it.

“Look at them. That little bird has been shot, clawed, and thrown into a wall — twice. The wolf-girl drank her last potion just to keep standing. That woman spent everything she had putting these people back together, and you bit her. And the man in the tunnel? He didn’t even get to fight today. They came up this mountain looking for a healer. And you did all this. Was it worth it, Warden?”

And the truly absurd thing — the thing the table will not let anyone forget — is that it works. Skathrix goes quiet. And then, bound and pinned and entirely unprompted, the ancient terror of the deep tunnels… starts explaining himself.

“You think I chose these tunnels? I was old before the Sovereign came. These deeps were MINE — my hunting grounds, my quiet, my dark. Then the storm arrived, and it was serve or be unmade. ‘Warden,’ he called me. A fang does not refuse the mouth it sits in. Do you know what it is, little grave-digger, to be leashed by something younger than your smallest scar?”

There is a pause. Somewhere in the cavern, someone whispers, “are we… bonding with it?” Lloyd nods along with terrible sincerity. “That’s hard. That’s genuinely hard, friend.”

For three full seconds, a behir and a paladin share a moment of mutual understanding in a lightning-scarred cavern. Then Skathrix remembers that he is being pitied — by a bound audience, on his own floor, with a wolf standing on his head — and the shame curdles into something much, much worse.

✦The Third Breath✦

The bindings hold for one heartbeat. Fury fills the next. Skathrix stops fighting the vines and simply ignites — lightning breath tearing loose with no warning at all, a wall of white scouring the cavern end to end. And in the same convulsion, his bound neck whips.

The rope snaps taut and comes along — Kenra wrenched off her feet — and Kree, diving back into the fight, catches the full arc of that massive skull. It slams him into the ceiling hard enough to crack crystal, holds him there one crushing instant, and lets him drop. Kenra sheds the rope and runs, wolf-shape stretched flat-out, and catches him on her own back before the stone can.

Thalasstheos has no one to catch her. The breath finds her mid-cast and takes her square. Her hands drop, and the vines wither to ash as her concentration breaks with her. She crumples to the cavern floor and does not move — arm flung out, hair spilled across the stone.

Lloyd takes the blast standing because there’s no time for anything else — driven to one knee, spade planted, coat smoking, gold light stuttering around him like a candle refusing the wind.

The behir shakes off the dead vines and rises to his full height over the wreckage of them. Free again. Furious. And very, very tired of introductions.

✦The Long Walk Up✦

Skathrix does not finish them with lightning. He does something worse. He dismisses them. The great serpent turns for the high tunnel, and he takes the long way — over the party, one by one, with all the unhurried contempt of a king crossing his own floor. A clawed foot the size of a cart wheel presses Lloyd flat into the stone. The next footfall grinds down on Kenra and Kree where the wolf still shields him. Each step is deliberate. Each step is a word in a sentence: You are beneath me.

Then he reaches Thalasstheos. The behir pauses over her small, still shape. His head lowers, almost gently — and the moment his jaws close around her, she wakes up fighting. She kicks, claws, wedges an arm against a fang the size of a fence post — braced crosswise in the serpent’s mouth through sheer refusal, held at the very edge of the swallow.

Kree hits the behir’s face like a thrown knife. Up off the floor, wings screaming, talons first — raking across Skathrix’s eyes, once, twice. The first furnace eye slams shut, weeping sparks. The second follows.

The Warden of the deep tunnels is blind.

The roar that comes out of him is raw, deafening pain — his head thrashing against the dark that has suddenly swallowed HIM, Thalasstheos still wedged behind his fangs, jamming ice into his tongue, kicking at the hinge of his jaw, fighting the pull of his throat inch by furious inch.

✦The Skewer✦

Lloyd peels himself off the crystal floor. Boot-printed. Half-crushed. Patient. He picks up his last javelin, watches the huge blind head swing back and forth — and puts everything into the throw. His arm. His shoulder. His god.

This one does not miss.

The javelin punches deep into the behir’s neck, and the Divine Smite goes off inside the wound — a muffled crack of gold light under the scales, like a bell rung underwater. Skathrix seizes. His whole body whips into the wall.

And then Lloyd does the strangest thing anyone has ever done in that cavern. He cups his hands and calls up to the blind, skewered serpent — voice steady, sorrowful, absolutely certain, the voice of an orc who has dug a hundred graves and knows exactly what dying sounds like:

“That was your heart, Warden. I felt it give. I’m sorry — it’s over. You’re already dead.”

The behir goes still. Blind, bleeding, a javelin of holy light buried in his neck and the evidence of his own senses in ruins — Skathrix Coilborn, ancient terror of the deep tunnels… believes him.

✦Walking Away✦

The great serpent sways once, twice — and lies down to die. It is, by any measure, a very committed performance. The coils slacken loop by loop. The sparking scales dim politely. The massive head settles onto the crystal floor with a theatrical, shuddering sigh, jaws going loose —

— and out of those jaws, soaked and shaking and incandescent with dignity, climbs Thalasstheos. She plants one foot on a fang, hauls herself over the serpent’s lip, slides down the side of his chin, and stands a moment on the cavern floor — dripping, catching her breath, smoothing her ruined hair back with both hands.

Nobody says anything. Wisely. She limps past the behir’s enormous “corpse” without giving it a glance. “We’re leaving.”

They gather themselves the way survivors do — slowly, in pieces. Lloyd retrieves his javelin with a workmanlike tug and a quiet word of thanks to Aurys. Kree collects his scattered arrows and his black sword, wings dragging. Kenra, still wearing the wolf, kneels at the tunnel mouth — and Wilhelm, rifle and all, is loaded gently onto her broad blue back like the world’s most exhausted saddlebag.

And so they go — a limping, boot-printed, half-dead procession climbing toward the storm above: a paladin using his spade as a crutch, a rogue walking because flying hurts, a healer with no healing left, and a wolf carrying an artificer who slept through the whole thing.

Behind them, one furnace eye cracks open a sliver — watching them go. Skathrix Coilborn, Warden of the deep tunnels, dead as requested, decides to stay that way.

Chapter Thirteen

The Heir of the Tempest

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

The tunnel climbs, and the storm climbs with it — the thunder no longer overhead but around, in the rock, in their teeth. Then the passage widens all at once, and the mountain opens. They step out onto a vast cliff face, and the world falls away into storm-wreathed distance. Clouds coil below them like a living sea. The wind howls hard enough to steal the breath out of their lungs, and there is nowhere left to climb and nowhere left to hide.

Lightning strikes. For one heartbeat the whole sky is lit — and Kree stops moving. Because he knows that shape. He has seen it before — one terrible glimpse from the camp below, half-buried in cloud, the thing he told himself was a trick of the storm. It is not a trick of the storm.

Massive cobalt wings unfurl against the boiling sky. Scales like polished sapphire catch the flash and hold it. A long, horned head turns — slowly, unhurried, the way nothing prey-sized ever moves — toward the tunnel mouth and the five small figures frozen in it.

The dragon does not roar. Not at first. It simply watches — with cold, electric intelligence. Then the great jaws part, and the roar comes — and it isn’t a beast’s sound at all. It rolls across the cliff in layers, and underneath the thunder of it there are words, or the shape of them, and the storm above wheels tighter in answer like a flock coming to heel.

Vaelthundrax, Heir of the Tempest, has decided to come down.

Wings wider than the cliff catch the gale. The dragon descends through his own storm and lands on the mountain’s face above them — talons shearing into rock, lightning crawling up his wings and into him, welcome — and looks down at what the tunnels have coughed up: a paladin leaning on a spade, a rogue trembling not entirely from cold, a wolf with an artificer on her back, and a healer with nothing left.

✦Before the Storm✦

One breath before the end of the world, Thalasstheos steps up beside Kree. The rogue’s wings are shaking. “I saw it. Before. At the camp. I thought — I told myself it was the storm.”

“It was.” She has no magic left, no plan, nothing but the knife at her belt and the people beside her. She looks at Kree. “You caught me. When it mattered. So aim like that.”

Kree’s talons steady on the bowstring — just enough.

✦The Losing Fight✦

Vaelthundrax opens with the mountain itself. One taloned forefoot rises and strikes the cliff — and the terrain becomes his. The shelf cracks like pond ice, whole slabs tilting and shearing away, and the lightning follows his will down into the stone: crawling veins of white fire racing through every fissure, turning the ground they stand on into a burning web where no step is safe.

The party scatters across a battlefield that is actively hunting them — and the dragon moves. His tail comes around almost lazily, a sweep of armored sapphire longer than the cliff is wide. Thalasstheos — mid-stumble on the splitting rock — takes it full across the body and simply leaves: flung like a doll, cracking against the cliff wall, crumpling into the rubble at its base.

Kenra howls and charges — and Vaelthundrax meets her with one foot. The stomp drives the great blue wolf flat into the burning stone, claws caging her, pressing.

Kree empties everything he has into the dragon’s face — arrow after arrow, screaming into the wind. Lloyd comes in under the wing with the spade blazing, smiting for the joint of the great foreleg with every oath he owns. The arrows skip off sapphire. The smite lands with a crack of gold — and Vaelthundrax does not even shift his weight.

The horned head swings down, slow and curious, to look at the two of them — the way one looks at bugs that have, adorably, decided to bite. “Is that all the Warden left of you?”

✦The Bite, Again✦

The dragon answers Lloyd’s smite personally. The horned head snaps down and the jaws close around him — armor, coat, and all — and Vaelthundrax shakes, violent whipping arcs that crack through the paladin’s body, the spade spinning away across the burning stone. Then, bored, the dragon opens his mouth mid-swing and lets him fly. Lloyd hits the cliff shelf once, bounces, and lands in a heap that doesn’t move.

By the wall, in the rubble, Thalasstheos comes back to herself — blood in her mouth, the world ringing. And she feels it — the rain. It is hammering the whole cliff, running down her face, her arms, pooling in every crack. Not a cavern. Not a crystal cage. Open sky — and the sea, as it always does, has come to her.

The water answers like it never left. A thin thread — she is scraped to the very bottom of herself — but a thread is enough for one thing. She drags herself to the crumpled paladin, presses both hands over his caved breastplate, and pours the last of the tide into him.

“Not yet, grave-digger. You still owe me for the poaching.”

Lloyd’s eyes snap open on a ragged gasp. Somewhere under the pain: “…Aurys keeps… accounts…” “So do I.” She hauls him up.

✦The Dance✦

The cliff is chaos in three places at once. Kree is a grey scrap in a hurricane — weaving, diving, bolts of directed lightning chasing him across the sky. Under the dragon’s foot, Kenra fights like a buried storm. Thalasstheos heals herself enough to hold together and runs for the pinned wolf — and never gets there. Vaelthundrax spins, tail and wing scything the shelf, and she hurls herself flat under the sweep and comes down prone in the open with nothing between her and the dragon at all.

Then Lloyd throws. His last javelin. No smite left, just an orc’s arm and every grave he’s ever dug behind it — and by whatever ledger Aurys keeps, this one counts. The point punches through dragonhide at the base of the wing — the first true wound of the fight — and a line of dark blood runs down the sapphire.

Vaelthundrax goes very still. The horned head turns — past Kree, past the wolf — and fixes on the two problems below: the healer prone on the open stone, and the paladin standing over his own throw, empty-handed.

The dragon starts toward them. Each step cracks the shelf. And the party’s two unbreakable hearts — break.

Thalasstheos does not get up. She can’t. She is flat on her belly on the burning stone, rain hammering her back, and every instinct that carried her through the Warden’s teeth has simply stopped answering. This close, the dragon isn’t a monster — it’s weather, it’s the sky deciding, and there is no arguing with the sky. The only sound out of her, the healer who talked back to a behir from inside its mouth, is a single, small, involuntary: “…no. no, no—”

Beside her, Lloyd is backing away. Lloyd. His empty hands are up — not raised in surrender, just up, the useless, ancient reflex of a creature putting something between itself and the end — and his boots are sliding backward one step at a time, and his face has gone grey under the green. He is not planning. He is not praying. There is no plan and the prayer won’t come.

And that is when Kree — out of arrows, out of altitude, out of every sensible idea — lands on a boulder in the dragon’s eyeline and starts to dance. Wings out. Talons stamping. Tail feathers shaking. A full, frantic, gloriously stupid aarakocra war-dance in the pouring rain, complete with hollering:

“HEY! HEY, SPARK-LIZARD! OVER HERE! LOOK AT ME! I’M SO INTERESTING! I HAVE SO MANY SHINY THINGS!”

✦The Fear✦

The dragon does not even glance at the dance. The great head comes down — slow, inevitable, rain streaming off the horns — and Lloyd, backed against nothing, spends the last coin he has. He plants his feet the way he did in the cavern below, raises one shaking hand, and puts the whole weight of his god into the word that turned a behir:

“STOP—”

The word breaks against Vaelthundrax like a wave against a cliff. The storm-cold eyes don’t flicker. The dragon’s lips peel back from rows of lightning-white teeth in something that is unmistakably, horribly, a grin — the first expression he has bothered to wear all night.

“No.”

And then he presses — not with claw or breath, but with everything he is — and terror rolls off him like the thunderclap after a strike. It is not an emotion. It is a force of nature with their names on it.

Thalasstheos shatters first. Something ancient and animal takes the wheel, and her body answers the only way a cornered druid’s body knows how. She wildshapes — not the wolf, not the bird — a snake, small and quick and low, the shape of pure escape — and she is gone, a silver-blue ribbon flowing over the cracked stone, away, away, away, running for her life without a single glance back.

Lloyd doesn’t run. Fear does something worse to him. He stands frozen — Command dead in his mouth, hands still raised — and watches the bravest person he has ever met flee as a snake through the rain, and the horror on his face isn’t for the dragon anymore.

That is the moment the pinned wolf finally gets her feet under the talon-cage. Kenra erupts — every ounce of dire wolf and buried fury releasing at once — and pounces, jaws closing on the dragon’s foreleg above the javelin wound. In the same heartbeat a grey blur abandons the boulder and the dance, and Kree hits the dragon’s face, talons raking for the cold electric eyes exactly the way he blinded the Warden below.

Vaelthundrax roars — and this time it is not for show.

✦The Storm’s Answer✦

The great neck arches back, wings flaring against his own storm — and the breath comes down on the cliff like the sky splitting open. Not the behir’s line of lightning. A flood of it, sweeping the shelf in a roaring arc, and there is no cover, and there is no luck, and it finds every single one of them.

Kree drops out of the air mid-scratch, wings seizing — smoke curling off his feathers. Lloyd, still frozen, takes it standing and goes down to both knees at last, armor glowing faintly at the seams.

The wolf on the dragon’s foreleg convulses — and the wolf ends. The wildshape bursts apart in a scatter of blue light, and it is small, feathered Kenra who tumbles down the sapphire scales and lands in the rubble, sickles somehow already back in her hands, too dazed to stand.

And the little snake fleeing through the rocks takes the edge of the arc mid-slither — and Thalasstheos, too, comes apart back into herself: thrown down among the broken slabs at the cliff’s rim, scorched again, gasping again, the fear still riding her heartbeat.

This time she doesn’t stand up into the storm. She hides. She drags herself into the black gap beneath two fallen slabs — pressed flat, rain sheeting past the opening, both hands over her own mouth — and through the crack she watches the lightning strobe over what’s left of them: Lloyd on his knees, Kree crumpled and smoking, Kenra swaying upright with two small sickles against a thing the size of the sky.

The Heir of the Tempest stalks forward through his own light, unhurried, wings half-spread, savoring the arithmetic of it.

Four scattered. One hidden. None standing but a dazed kenku girl.

The storm leans in.

✦The Last Stand✦

Down in the rubble, something unclenches in Thalasstheos’s chest. Through the crack between the slabs she can see where the dragon is walking — and it is walking toward Wilhelm, still slumped where Kenra left him, unconscious, unguarded, unaware.

She fled once tonight. She does not do it twice.

She crawls out of the dark, plants her knees in the wet stone, and forces her breathing flat the way the tide goes flat before it turns. Focus. Focus. The clouds above the dragon split — and a pillar of silver light drops out of his own storm and lands square across his face. Moonbeam. Vaelthundrax recoils a half-step, light searing across the sapphire scales, and his head whips around to find the little healer kneeling in the open, hands raised, still here.

Lloyd’s head comes up. Still on his knees, armor smoking at the seams — and he drives the shovel into the stone and hauls himself upright over it, and golden light spills out from where he stands, a wide ring of it burning steady against the rain. Inside that circle the terror thins. Kree drags himself out of the rubble into the light. Kenra stops swaying. The paladin holds the warding there with his teeth gritted, concentration a physical weight on his shoulders, and around him what’s left of the company gets up off the ground.

Arrows start leaving Kree’s bow — one, two, steady, spiteful. Thalasstheos runs the gap to Kenra, water already glowing in her palms, and the wounds close under her hands even as the moonbeam’s pillar tracks across the shelf after the circling dragon.

They lean their heads together for one breath, and they make a plan.

Then all of them go at once.

Kenra’s wildshape roars back to life mid-sprint — a wolf again, jaws wide — and she leaps and buries her teeth in the dragon’s throat. Kree hits the face a second time, claws finding the eyes he’s already marked once tonight. Thalasstheos flings a hand out, and Faerie Fire crawls over the great body in guttering violet light, every scale suddenly outlined, nowhere to hide. And Lloyd comes in low with a shout, spinning the whole weight of his body around the shovel like it’s a warhammer, and rings it off the dragon’s jaw.

For one held breath — it is working.

✦The Storm’s Verdict✦

Vaelthundrax has had enough.

The wings slam down and a gust like a solid wall bursts outward — and the whole party goes to its knees at once, wolf and bird and paladin and healer flattened against the stone. Then the lightning comes chaining — leaping from Lloyd to Kree to Kenra to Thalasstheos, one white arc threading them all like beads on a wire, and the shelf strobes with it.

The wolf is still between him and the sky, and the dragon is furious now. A talon wrenches a slab of sharp rock loose from the cliff itself — edges like broken glass, half the size of a door — and he hurls it at Kenra.

Kenra doesn’t see it. She is mid-snarl, blood on her muzzle, eyes on the throat she bit. She never sees it.

Thalasstheos does.

There is no decision. That’s the thing the others will argue about later, in the quiet, when arguing is easier than grieving — whether she chose it. But there’s no weighing in her face, no hesitation in her stride. She is simply moving, the moment the stone leaves the talon, the way water moves downhill — as if some part of her had already worked out, long ago, without telling the rest of her, what she would spend herself on if it came to it.

She has spent the whole campaign putting herself between this party and the dark. Every wound, every watch. Hands glowing over Kree’s torn wing on the manor wall. Kneeling over Lloyd in the cave with the behir’s teeth-marks still in her own shoulder. Pulling fevers out through her palms on the long wet road. She has done it so many times that none of them see her do it anymore. It is simply what Thalasstheos is: the person standing between.

She gets there.

The rock takes her full in the chest, in front of the wolf, and the sound of it is something none of them will describe out loud, ever. The force lifts her off her feet — gently, almost, from far away it might look gentle — and carries her backward through the rain, past the edge of the cliff, and out into the open dark.

For one instant she hangs there against the storm, arms wide, hair a banner of silver-blue — lit white by the lightning, like something the sky is taking back.

Then she is gone.

Kenra’s howl and Lloyd’s shout land on empty air. Kree is already diving — wings smoking, seizing, failing — and the wind wall slams him back onto the shelf like a toy. There is nothing to catch her. There is no one left who can.

✦The Fall✦

Thalasstheos falls with her eyes open.

The cliff face slides upward past her, faster and faster, and the strange thing — the thing she would tell them, if she could — is that she isn’t afraid. The fear that rode her heartbeat all night, the fear that made her a snake, the fear she is so ashamed of — it stayed up there on the cliff, with the dragon. It doesn’t fall with her.

The rain falls with her instead. Thousands of drops, keeping pace, falling exactly as fast as she is — so that in the middle of falling it looks, impossibly, like floating. Like the whole sky decided to come down with her so she wouldn’t do it alone.

She thinks of them. Not in a rush — slowly, one at a time, like laying shells in a row. Kree, who caught her once when the behir dropped her, who will not forgive himself for not being fast enough twice. Kenra, safe — safe, the rock did not touch her, and that thought sits in Thalasstheos’s chest warmer than the wound. Wilhelm, asleep through all of it, who will wake up and have to be told. Lloyd, who heals with his hands the way she does, who she was jealous of for one small ugly moment in a cave — she hopes someone tells him it wasn’t ugly for long.

Her hands are glowing faintly, out of habit. Even now. Even falling. Hands that have closed a hundred wounds reaching for one they cannot reach.

She lets them rest.

The sea is somewhere below her in the dark — she can smell it through the rain, salt and cold and enormous, the oldest smell she knows. Genasi children are told the tide never loses anything. It goes out, and it looks like leaving, and it is not leaving. It is the sea reaching for its own.

The tide goes out, she thinks. The tide comes back.

For a strange, quiet moment it feels less like falling and more like being carried — the way the sea holds what it takes. She feels, at the end of everything, at peace.

Above her, small and far away and getting farther, the platform gives way at last — lightning, tail, stone shearing from the mountain — and the rest of them are falling too, rubble and rain and company together into the dark.

The party falls.

The ground rushes up to meet them —

✦The Clearing✦

The world comes back to Kenra as birdsong and the specific, unhurried weight of a small bird standing on her beak.

They regard each other, upside down, for a long moment — the sparrow entirely unbothered, the kenku flat on her back in a bald patch of grass with a forest standing quietly around her — and then it flicks its wings and is gone, as if it had only stopped by to confirm she was interesting. She sits up. The clearing is small and improbably gentle: soft grass, ringed in trees, scattered with things that have no business in it — splintered planks of mountain platform, a fist of scorched stone, half a supply crate — the storm’s litter, dropped from a great height along with two druids’ worth of luck. Kree is sprawled ten feet away, feathers askew, one wing over his face like a gentleman refusing morning.

They get up the way people get up when they can’t believe they’re able to: one joint at a time, inventorying. Trees on all sides. A small water basin catching the grey light, its surface still dimpling with the last of the rain. No cliff. No dragon. No storm — just its wreckage, and the enormous, suspicious quiet of being alive.

They drink, and they walk — and the forest answers with a sound that drops them both into a crouch: a single, ground-shaking thud, somewhere ahead.

What they find in the broken bracken is Skathrix.

The Warden of the deep tunnels lies where the mountain finally set him down, vast and dark and stone-still, the long serpent body folded wrong among snapped saplings. Whether the lightning took him, or the collapse, or the fall itself, no one will ever say — but the behir who dragged healers to ceilings and swallowed his debts is dead as the stones around him, and the two smallest members of the company stand over him for a moment in something that is not quite grief and not quite triumph.

Then, because they are an alchemist’s apprentice and a scout with a working knowledge of what things are worth: they get out the knives. Horns. Teeth — the long front ones, carefully. A wide sheet of storm-grey hide. Skathrix pays his last debt in trophies, and Kenra and Kree move on down the slope, considerably heavier and considerably richer.

✦The Cave Mouth✦

Lloyd wakes in the mouth of a cave with rain-mist on his boots and a stranger beside him.

The stranger wakes seconds later, and immediately demonstrates the problem with him: volume. Wilhelm — artificer, recently unconscious through the entire dragon — surfaces mid-sentence, as if resuming an argument, and finds himself in an unknown cave next to an enormous armored orc he has never once seen in his life.

“—who — where — who are you? WHERE IS MY— what have you done with my party—”

“Friend,” Lloyd tries, palms out. “Easy. I’m a friend of—”

“I DON’T KNOW YOU!” Wilhelm announces, at a pitch that bounces off three walls and out over the whole rain-soaked valley, advertising their position to every surviving thing on the mountainside. “KREE?! KENRA?! THERE’S AN ORC—”

Lloyd, paladin of Aurys, weigher of oaths, looks at the shouting man. He looks out at the valley. He performs a brief moral calculation, closes one fist, and — with the same clean economy he swings a shovel — knocks Wilhelm out cold.

“Forgive me,” he tells the slumped artificer, entirely sincerely, hoisting him over one shoulder. “You’ll thank me. Or you won’t.”

He walks on.

✦The Blue Thing✦

On the trail below the clearing, Kenra has discovered the mushrooms.

They are, in her defense, exceptional mushrooms — fat and cream-capped, in polite little congregations along the deadfall — and the company’s stores are at the bottom of a mountain somewhere, so this is foraging, which is responsible, and she is on her knees filling a trophy-hide bundle with them when Kree calls out from up the trail.

“Kenra.” A pause. “Kenra — blue.”

That word, from that voice, closes the distance in her chest before her feet even move. Snagged on a thorn-break at the trail’s edge, fluttering: blue. Not sky-blue, not flower-blue — her blue, sea-over-sand blue — and beneath the thorns, half-buried in wet leaves, the satchel. Thalasstheos’s satchel, the one that has hung at the healer’s hip since Brindlewick, strap snapped, flap muddied, contents rattling when Kree lifts it with both hands like something that might still be attached to its owner.

It isn’t. That’s the whole terrible weight of it.

They look up the slope, and down it. Rain-mist, trees, silence. Kree slings the satchel across his chest, and they walk faster now — though Kenra still stops, every little while, for the mushrooms. Later she will explain this, and be right: she was going to need something to come back to.

✦The Trail of Blue✦

Wilhelm wakes the second time mid-thrash, upside down over a moving mountain of orc.

“PUT ME— down, put me down, DOWN—”

Lloyd, who has carried complaining cargo before, sets him down with the patience of a man returning a library book. And Wilhelm, upright and furious and drawing breath for the full speech — stops.

Because past Lloyd’s elbow, caught on the rocks at the treeline: a torn piece of blue fabric.

He knows the blue. His feet know it before he does; he’s moving without deciding to, Lloyd falling in behind — and the mountainside gives them the rest one piece at a time, the way bad news is always given. Another torn ribbon of robe. A smear on wet stone that the rain has not managed to wash away. A trail — drag-marked, rust-dark, terribly short — leading down through the rocks to the shelter of an overhang.

And there she is.

Thalasstheos lies at the end of the blood trail, small and still, robe torn and scorched, one hand open toward the sky as if she’d been holding something the fall took from her. Wilhelm reaches her at a stumbling run and drops to his knees, and everything the artificer is — the instruments, the training, the clever measuring hands — goes to work in a fury: pulse. Breath. Warmth. The clever hands try everything, twice, three times, faster, and the mountain lets him have his answer.

Nothing. Cold. Gone.

Wilhelm kneels there a moment with his friend’s hand in his. Then he stands, turns, and levels his gun at the orc.

“You.” The barrel doesn’t waver; his voice does. “I wake up next to a stranger and my healer is — what did you do—”

Lloyd doesn’t reach for the shovel. He looks at the gun, and past it, at the man breaking behind it, and his voice comes out the way it does across a job-board table: level, unhurried, true.

“I never met her. I came up this mountain to save her — got hired off a notice board to do it, and I was too late, and that’ll sit on me as long as I live. But I fought beside your friends last night, and buried nobody, and I am not starting today.” A breath. “Point it somewhere useful, or help me carry her home.”

The gun holds a moment longer — then drops, because grief needs both hands.

Wilhelm carries her himself. He will not be argued with. Down through the rocks with Thalasstheos cold in his arms, held close against the rain the way you hold something you are ashamed of having slept through losing — and the trees finally break open onto a riverside, wide and grey and loud with snowmelt.

✦The Riverbank✦

The other bank has people on it.

Kenra sees them first and shouts — a raw, doubled sound, half her own — and Kree’s cry goes up beside it, and Wilhelm lifts his head and sees, across the water, two impossible, incongruous smears of color against the grey: rust-red and storm-feather black. Alive. Alive. He walks toward them like a man wading, the river shallows soaking him to the knee and not slowing him at all.

And then they are all on one bank, and the shouting dies.

Because they see what Wilhelm is carrying. And the reunion the mountain owed them arrives broken in half: four survivors, standing solemn in a ring in the rain, around the one who made sure of it.

It’s the satchel that breaks the stillness. Wilhelm’s eyes land on it — slung across Kree’s chest — and something ignites behind them.

“Give me that. Quickly.”

He doesn’t wait; he snatches it, drops to his knees beside her, and digs — and from the bottom of the healer’s satchel he draws out the tablet: the old stone slab, dense with grooves no one could read, that has ridden at the bottom of her bag past every campfire question about it. He sets it flat on Thalasstheos.

“Spike. There’s a — the wound, it’s still in her, help me—” His hands are already moving, drawing the torn robe back from the wound the rock left, and there — deep, dark, a splinter of the cliff itself still seated in her like a key in a lock. Wilhelm sets his jaw, grips it with an artificer’s precision, and pulls it free.

The tablet answers.

Light runs the carved grooves like water finding channels — quick, silver-blue, tidal — and the whole slab flares once, softly, the way the sea flares under a first star. And then, its message delivered, it simply lets go: the stone sighs into dust between one heartbeat and the next, sifting away through Wilhelm’s fingers, off her body, into the rain.

And Thalasstheos draws breath.

The first one is small — a tide turning in the smallest possible harbor — and the second is a ragged, whooping gasp, and her eyes flutter, and the riverbank detonates. Kenra is airborne. Kree is making a sound no one will ever get him to admit to. Wilhelm is laughing and crying at the same tempo, and Lloyd — the stranger who climbed a mountain for a name on a poster — stands over the whole soaked, screaming, miraculous pile of them with rain on his face, watching the party he was hired to save resurrect itself into his arms.

They are battered, bankrupt, half-naked, lost, and alive. All five. All alive.

Slowly — leaning on each other in a chain, the healer carried gentler than glass at the center of it — they pick themselves up, and keep hobbling forward.
