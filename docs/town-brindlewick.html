The Eldermarch Gazetteer

Brindlewick

A quiet mill-town on the River Brindle — bread, brass, and the last warm beds before the Wood. Where it all began.

Brindlewick is a modest mill-town straddling the slow, brown River Brindle. Three old waterwheels turn its mills; cobbled lanes wind between timber roofs gone dark with damp; woodsmoke and baking bread hang in the air at all hours. It is the last stop before the Phandalin road bends into the Wood — the kind of town people pass through rather than toward.

It is where the company's story began — and it is known, quietly, for two things: bread and brass.

✦Bread & Brass

Bread first: the mills make this a grain town, and the riverside bakeries' Sunday loaf is good enough that Phandalin merchants detour for it. Brass second: small workshops that fix clockwork, repair tools, and tinker. Between the two, Brindlewick produces useful, unglamorous people rather than heroes. Its motto is carved over the market arch: “Bread baked, debts paid, doors shut by dark.”

✦The Rain-Omen

Brindlewick sits in an oddly dry pocket of the valley — rain is genuinely rare here, especially in autumn. Locals read it like tea leaves. A wet morning is “rain-luck”: some say it washes off the old and begins something new; the nervous say rain only falls on Brindlewick when something elsewhere has gone wrong. Whether the dry pocket is mere weather or something older, no two locals agree.

The morning the company left town, it rained.

✦Trouble on the Lanes

For weeks before the company left, bandits had grown thick on the roads out of town. “Doors shut by dark” is becoming less a saying and more a rule, and the Reeve would very much like the problem solved quietly, before trade reroutes around the town.

✦Faces of Note

Reeve Aldous PenhallowThe closest thing Brindlewick has to a mayor — keeper of the town ledger and the night-bell. Soft-spoken, orderly, allergic to trouble.
Master Tobias QuintWilhelm's old mentor at the Cogwright Academy. Proud and dry, and quietly stung that his best tinker walked off to go adventuring.
Old Maddie BramsellMatron of the bakery that makes the legendary Sunday loaf. She knows everyone's business because everyone buys bread. Gruff, generous, free with day-old crusts for the children.
Garrick VaneA retired caravan guard who now keeps the Drowned Lantern. Firm opinions about the lanes and the bandits, and old contacts from the road.
Nessa SellwrightThe sharp young tinker who runs the town's brass-and-repair shop — Wilhelm's friendly rival.
The Drub BrothersThe millers who control the three waterwheels and bicker endlessly over water rights — touchy, in a town that barely sees rain.
Old Pell, the Rain-WatcherAn eccentric who keeps a decades-long “rain ledger” and mutters omens at passers-by. Possibly more right than anyone cares to admit.
Marta BrinThe merchant the company rescued and first worked for, ferrying goods between Brindlewick and Phandalin. Grateful and reliable.

✦Places of Note

  • The Market Arch — The town's heart, its motto carved overhead. Market days, the notice board, public announcements.
  • Bramsell's Bakery (Bread Row) — The smell that defines Brindlewick; rumors, free crusts for children, cheap day-old loaves.
  • The Three Wheels — The working mills on the Brindle.
  • The Brindle Landing — The barge dock where grain goes downriver and Marta's wagons load.
  • The Cogwright Academy — A modest stone hall of practical artifice and letters: workshops, a small library, and Wilhelm's locked-up old room above it.
  • The Drowned Lantern — Ironically named for water in a dry town; Garrick's inn — warm, welcoming, the best beds before the Wood.
  • Sellwright's Fixery — Brass, clockwork, tools, and oddments; Nessa's workshop.
  • The Dry Well — A famously waterless well in the square; townsfolk drop a coin and wish for rain, or against it.
  • The Old Toll Road — The eastern road out of town toward Phandalin Wood — where the bandits have been thickest.
