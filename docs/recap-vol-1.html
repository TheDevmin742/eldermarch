Chapter I

Session One — Brindlewick to Phandalin Road (Recap)

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

Autumn in Brindlewick. The town is small and ordinary: a river, cobbled streets, smoke from bakeries, and folk going about chores. The sky is low and the air smells of woodsmoke and wet earth.

Wilhelm wakes and goes about his morning ritual—collecting tools and locking up his small room above the academy. He hums to himself, making mental notes for the short, peaceful break he intends to take during the autumn.

Nearby, Kree perches above the market square, watching. Bright feathers flash as he studies the people below: merchants haggling, children chasing one another. He likes shiny things and watches the town’s movements with careful interest.

Rain begins to fall — odd, because Brindlewick rarely sees rain this time of year. Wilhelm pauses at his doorway, looks up, and says aloud, slightly puzzled, “How odd.” The sound of rainfall quickly becomes the background to the day.

✦Trouble in the Square✦

A sudden commotion rips through the square: two rough-looking bandits have accosted a merchant, demanding coin and threatening his stall. Villagers scatter. Kree swoops down to investigate, talons bared and eyes cold.

One bandit draws a knife; the merchant cries out. As Kree lands between the bandits and the merchant, his vivid feathers and quick movement make the attackers overconfident. They strike.

The fight is short and messy.

Kree slashes with his talons; one bandit staggers.

Wilhelm, who had been nearby, fumbles for his flintlock, aims, and fires. The first bandit falls, dead.

The second bandit manages to strike Kree with a dagger. Wilhelm fires again but misses, the shot thudding into a barrel. The bandit moves in to investigate.

While the bandit is distracted, Kree finds a potato in the spilled contents of the barrel and, impossibly, eats it mid-fight.

Wilhelm recovers his aim and fires point-blank; the bandit collapses.

After the dust settles, the square is a scene of quiet shock. The merchant, Marta Brin, breathes like she can finally stand again. Her hands shake as she clasps theirs.

“Thank you,” she says. “Truly. Bandits have been hitting the lanes for weeks. If two able people would escort my caravan through Phandalin Wood, I’ll pay fifty gold. It’s dangerous otherwise.”

Kree’s eyes light at the mention of coin. He doesn’t bother with ceremony — wings ruffling, he steps forward and grins in that quick, breathy way. “We’ll do it,” he says simply. “When do we leave?”

Wilhelm straightens, fingers tightening on the flintlock at his hip. He opens his mouth to refuse. “I—” he begins, voice small and uncomfortable. “I’m not a traveler. I teach at the academy. I have…responsibilities here.”

Marta’s face creases with worry. “Please. This shipment is important. Fifty gold now, more on delivery.” She glances between them. Kree looks like he’s already counting the coin in his head. Wilhelm hesitates, then forces out, “I cannot—” and then, with a resigned sigh and a practical glance at the gold, “—very well. I will go. For the money.”

Marta nods, clutching the coin pouch like a talisman. “Thank you. I’ll leave at first light.”

As Marta gathers herself and departs, the square slowly returns to its regular rhythm. Kree and Wilhelm are left standing in the muddy street, two strangers who have agreed to the same small and awkward bargain.

They walk to the nearest tavern together—not because either trusts the other, but because the rain has begun to soak through and a warm drink sounded better than walking home. Inside, the tavern’s lamplight throws a yellow glow over rough wooden tables and the smell of stew.

❖TAVERN❖
— Didn’t happen but its fun roleplay —

Kree perches on a bench, one wing half-folded. He drops a small, dull coin on the table, then watches Wilhelm with a curious tilt of his head. His voice is scratchy and quick.

“I’m Kree,” he says. “Thats pretty cool–” said Wilhelm awkwardly.

Wilhelm slides into the opposite seat, wiping rain from his sleeves. He gives a polite, guarded smile. “Wilhelm Thorne. I teach at the academy. I mend things.” He keeps the words clipped; he’s not eager to make conversation.

Kree laughs softly, the sound like feathers rustling. “Mend things? Good. Things break on the road.” He taps the coin. “Fifty’s a tidy sum. You look nervous.” He eyes the holster. “Are you a fighter or a tinkerer?”

Wilhelm’s shoulders tighten. “Tinkerer. Not a fighter. I have a flintlock because this is a world with trouble.” He forces a dry, embarrassed chuckle. “I am not—well. I have a life here. I didn’t plan to leave it.”

Kree studies him for a moment, then shrugs. “Plans change. Roads pay. And trouble finds those who stand still.” He leans forward, quick and bright. “Don’t worry. I know how to keep us quiet. You keep us clever.” He gestures vaguely at Wilhelm’s tools. “You fix things when we break them.”

Wilhelm lets a corner of his mouth lift. “I suppose that is one way of putting it.” He unwraps a small, paper-wrapped bundle — a neat, dry roll of bread Marta gave him — and offers a bite to the table rather than Kree. Kree snatches one, nibbling it with impatient pleasure. “You’re an odd bird,” Wilhelm says, not unkindly.

“And you’re a dour one,” Kree says back, grinning. “We might be alright together.” He wiggles his fingers: “We leave at dawn?”

Wilhelm looks at the coin, then at Kree’s expectant face, and exhales. “Dawn. We leave at dawn.” He pockets his reluctance with the same mechanical efficiency he uses to stow tools.

Kree claps once, pleased, and tips an imaginary hat. “Good. We’ll make sure your caravan gets to Phandalin.”

They drink, exchange a few practical details about the route, and fall into an uneasy, first-evening companionship — the kind that can be built fast on shared purpose and coin. The tavern’s warmth wraps around them, and outside the rain eases into a patter that seems, for a moment, less strange and more like the world settling back into itself.

Chapter II

The Road: Caravan to Phandalin

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

An hour into the escort, the caravan rolls along a shaded lane into the Whispering Pines. The forest is quiet; birds hush as the wagons pass. Kree, scouting ahead from a low branch, notices movement: wolves trailing the scent of the carriage.

“Kree, wolves,” he calls, voice a low trill. He lands and talks it over with Wilhelm.

Wilhelm squints at the tracks and uses his investigative eye to spot a faint, overgrown track that cuts through bracken—an old path that’s easier to hide the wagons on. “If we take this, the wolves might lose interest,” he says. Kree nods and hops up, pleased at the plan.

They follow the hidden route, and for now the wolves fall away.

✦Dusk — Camp and an Encounter✦

As dusk falls Wilhelm decides it’s best to make camp. The fire smells of wet wood. They spread a few blankets and keep watch.

The night air hums softly with insects, and the trees loom close around their little campfire. After the long day — fighting, arguing, and agreeing to guard a caravan neither truly wanted — both Wilhelm and Kree are bone-tired.

They sit by the faint firelight. Wilhelm has his coat draped over his shoulders, fussing absently with a brass tool he keeps turning in his hand. Kree, half-perched on a fallen log, yawns and stretches his wings lazily, feathers glinting with ember-light.

A twig snaps somewhere beyond the trees.

Kree tilts his head, his eyes narrowing. “You heard that?”

Wilhelm looks up, weary. “Probably a deer.”

Another sound — closer this time.

Wilhelm sighs, standing. “Fine. Let’s check before it decides we’re dinner.”

They move a few paces from the fire into the undergrowth, careful but sluggish with fatigue. The moon filters through branches, silvering the ferns underfoot.

❖THE UH-OH MOMENT❖

After a long, tense moment of silence, Wilhelm mutters, “Well… this clearing is indeed a clearing.”

Kree smirks, about to reply — and then freezes. From the trees ahead, an armed man steps into the moonlight. His clothes are rough, his eyes darting, and his hand rests near a blade. He looks like he hasn’t slept in days.

He doesn’t raise his weapon; instead, he gives a curt nod, scanning them both. Then, almost under his breath, he asks,

“Are you here for the job?”

Wilhelm blinks, throat dry. “What—what job?” he stammers.

The man’s eyes narrow instantly. “Who are you?” he growls, suspicion sharpening his tone.

Wilhelm panics. His hand jerks toward his flintlock. “I—uh—sorry—” he blurts, and fires before he even fully raises it.

The shot cracks through the night — BANG! — splintering bark beside the bandit’s head. Birds scatter from the treetops.

For a second, everyone just stares in stunned silence — Kree’s feathers puffed, the bandit blinking at the smoking tree behind him, Wilhelm’s hands shaking as the pistol drops slightly.

Then the bandit snarls and draws his blade.

“You’ll pay for that!”

Kree steps between them, daggers flashing. “Wonderful,” he mutters. “Can’t even camp one night without making friends.”

The fight is on.

The skirmish is rapid: the bandit slashes Wilhelm and knocks him forward; Kree drives his blade into the attacker and the man cries out for help. More bandits rush in, surrounding Kree.

Then—water.

A sudden torrent lashes out, slamming into the bandits and sending them sprawling, soaked and incapacitated. A colossal bluish bear scoops the prone attackers and hauls them into the underbrush, then shifts form. A woman stands where the bear had been: calm, radiant, with an air that hushes the forest. She kneels and presses her hands to Wilhelm and Kree.

The forest still hums with the echo of battle — the soft patter of rain through leaves, the smell of smoke, the faint hiss of Wilhelm’s spent pistol cooling in the mud. The bandits lie unconscious where a wave of water struck them down, their torches sputtering in the damp earth.

From the mist beyond the trees steps a figure — not a beast now, but a woman. Droplets trail off her hair like strands of liquid light. Her eyes, the deep blue of the sea at dusk, meet theirs with gentle certainty.

She kneels beside Wilhelm, who winces as she touches his arm. Her fingers are cool and faintly luminous, like moonlight reflected on still water.

“You are hurt,” she says softly. Her voice ripples like a calm tide, soothing even before her magic begins.

A shimmer of water curls from her hand, forming a small sphere that bursts into motes of light. The bruises and cuts fade, leaving only damp skin and astonished silence. Even the air seems cleaner, as if the forest itself had taken a long breath.

With a graceful motion, she traces a circle in the air. Sparks bloom from her fingertips, gathering into a steady, contained bonfire. Its warmth paints the clearing in gold and orange, chasing away the chill.

Then she sits cross-legged by the flames and smiles faintly.

“My name is Thalasstheos,” she says, her tone as calm as a still lake. “A druid of the Circle of the Sea. The forest whispered of danger, so I came. You’ve nothing to fear now.”

Kree blinks, still catching his breath. “A druid, huh?” he rasps. “Good timing. Thought we were fish food for a second there.”

Thalasstheos chuckles quietly, the sound soft as rain.

“The tides bring help where it is needed,” she replies. “Even to those who do not ask.”

She glances between them — the shaken artificer and the exhausted aarakocra — and adds gently,

“Rest. The forest keeps its peace tonight.”

✦Scene — Introductions by Firelight✦

The rain has stopped. Only the soft crackle of the bonfire breaks the night’s silence. The flames shimmer in Thalasstheos’s eyes like starlight reflecting off the ocean. Kree crouches near the fire, wings folded close, picking crumbs of hard bread from his pouch. Wilhelm sits opposite, carefully cleaning the barrel of his flintlock — more to steady his nerves than to actually polish it.

Thalasstheos watches the two for a while before breaking the quiet.

“It’s rare,” she says gently, “to find kindness in the woods after dark. I would know the names of those I share a fire with.”

Kree looks up, crumbs still clinging to his beak.

“Name’s Kree,” he says in a scratchy, birdlike voice. “Just a traveler. I don’t much care for towns — or for people who live in ‘em.”

He flicks a small stone into the fire, watching the sparks.

“Used to live high in the mountains with my flock. War came, and… well, there’s nothing left to go back to. Been flying ever since, wherever the wind or a bit of coin takes me.”

His tone wavers between pride and fatigue, like someone trying not to show how lonely the wind can be.

Thalasstheos nods slowly, her expression soft.

“The sky can be a lonely home,” she says. “But it seems it has given you strong wings.”

Kree shrugs but gives a faint, rasping chuckle.

“Maybe. Strong enough to land me here, I guess.”

Wilhelm clears his throat quietly, still not meeting either of their eyes.

“Wilhelm Thorne,” he says. “I, uh… teach at Bramblewick’s academy. Or, well, taught. I suppose I still do. I just needed a bit of quiet during the break.”

He sets the flintlock aside, hesitating before adding,

“I fix things. Always have. Machines, toys, bits of clockwork that no one else bothers with. Guess I just like… order. Metal doesn’t lie to you like people do.”

Kree tilts his head, one eye gleaming in the firelight.

“So why’d you bring that?” he asks, nodding to the pistol.

Wilhelm stiffens slightly.

“Habit,” he says simply. “You never know when something might break — or attack.”

There’s an awkward pause before Thalasstheos smiles kindly.

“You mend what is broken, then,” she says softly. “That is a noble craft. The world needs those who repair what others destroy.”

Wilhelm looks away, a faint pink in his cheeks.

“Not sure about noble,” he mutters. “But… thank you.”

Thalasstheos places a hand over her heart and bows her head slightly.

“I am called Thalasstheos,” she says. “Born of the sea, though I walk the land. I follow the path of the druid — to heal, to protect, and to remind the world that balance can always be restored. The waters taught me that all things flow, even pain.”

Kree blinks. “You talk like a poet,” he says.

She laughs softly. “The sea is full of poetry if you listen.”

For a time, they sit in peaceful silence. The fire pops; night creatures stir in the forest. Finally, Thalasstheos speaks again.

“If you two are bound for Phandalin, I would travel with you. The forest is restless of late. Safer if we walk together.”

Kree immediately nods. “Fine by me. You saved our tails back there — you’ve earned a spot.”

Wilhelm hesitates, looking between them, then sighs.

“If it keeps us from being mauled again, then yes. I suppose… company might not be so bad.”

Thalasstheos smiles, the firelight catching the shimmer of her hair.

“Then it is settled,” she says softly. “Tomorrow, we follow the river’s song — together.”

The night grows quiet again, the stars reflected faintly in her eyes. For the first time, Wilhelm doesn’t mind the silence.

Chapter III

Morning — The Farm and the Ashlings

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

On the second day they come across an abandoned farmhouse. It looks like no one has lived there for months. Curiosity and caution pull them inside.

Wilhelm’s eye catches two small white pinpricks of light in the shadow—tiny eyes watching. A small, black, ash-like creature hops out and squeaks in a voice too high for normal ears. Thalasstheos hears it plainly.

Kree and Wilhelm are confused. Kree urges Thalasstheos to translate; she obliges.

Thalasstheos (softly): “He says his name is Bop. He asks if you are friends.”

Thalasstheos kneels beside the soot-streaked hearth, her hands open and welcoming. A faint glow flickers beneath the ashes — and soon, a tiny figure climbs out, no taller than her hand, glowing like a coal in moonlight. It lets out a burst of squeaks, high-pitched and quick, like the chirping of a cricket mixed with the hiss of steam.

Kree squints. “Uh… is it saying something?”

Thalasstheos smiles softly, eyes still on the creature. “He calls himself Bop,” she says, voice low and warm. “He’s their leader — or tries to be.”

Bop squeaks again, arms flailing as little flecks of ash scatter through the air.

“He says…” Thalasstheos translates, pausing as she listens, “…they once lived in a great hearth. A home full of warmth and song. But people came — tore down their fire, smothered it, and threw them out. They wandered until they found this farmhouse, hoping to hide here until they could find a place of their own.”

The group follows her up to the second story. There’s a sudden whoosh from the chimney, and ash rains down as dozens of tiny shapes spill out — glowing faintly orange and gold, squeaking and chittering in fright. They cling to rafters and walls, their light pulsing like candle flames in a storm.

Thalasstheos watches them with quiet empathy. “There are many,” she murmurs. “Too many to keep running.”

Bop squeaks again, his tone pleading, hands clasped together.

“He asks for our help,” Thalasstheos says softly. “They’re being driven away by the nearby farmers who fear fire. They just want somewhere warm — somewhere safe.”

For a moment, only the sound of wind through the shutters fills the farmhouse.

Kree folds her wings, skeptical. “And what exactly do you expect us to do? Build them a chimney?”

Thalasstheos’s gaze sharpens, her blue eyes shimmering like moonlight on still water. “I expect us to be kind,” she says. “The world has plenty of destroyers. It could use a few more menders.”

Wilhelm sighs, rubbing the back of his neck. “Fine. But if one of those little guys gets into my powder bag, we’re going to have a real problem.”

Thalasstheos translates, and Bop squeaks joyfully — a staccato melody of gratitude that fills the room like laughter made of firelight.

Thalasstheos: “They mean no harm. I can help, but they are vulnerable. Will you stand with them?”

❖LEAVING THE FARMHOUSE❖

Wilhelm, ever the problem-solver, rubs his temples as he eyes the growing crowd of glowing ashlings. “Forty of them,” he mutters. “And not a single cage or crate.”

After a long moment, his gaze lands on the caravan. “...The wagon,” he says suddenly. “If we line a barrel with damp cloth and keep it covered, they’ll stay together — safe and contained.”

He sets to work before anyone can object, hauling a barrel down with efficient determination. Bop squeaks nervously as Wilhelm gestures toward the ashlings, motioning for them to hop in.

Kree’s feathers puff in alarm. “Whoa, whoa—no! They’re not cargo, Wilhelm!” He steps between him and the barrel, wings spread protectively. “You can’t just—just stuff them in there!”

Wilhelm freezes mid-motion, eyes darting between her and the tiny creatures. “It’s not like that,” he mumbles, defensive but weary. “They’ll be safe. It’s just practical.”

Thalasstheos places a gentle hand on the rim of the barrel. Her voice is calm, but carries the quiet strength of flowing water. “Practicality is good,” she says softly, “but compassion is better. We do not cage what has trusted us. Let them choose to ride with us — not be carried like sacks of grain.”

The water genasi kneels and murmurs something in the old tongue of the waves. The ashlings, glowing faintly, begin to chirp and clamber willingly into the wagon, nestling among blankets and embers.

Thalasstheos smiles, meeting Wilhelm’s gaze. “See? Even flame travels easier when it feels safe.”

Wilhelm exhales through his nose — half annoyed, half impressed. “Fine,” he mutters, adjusting the reins. “But if they set my wheel grease on fire, you’re explaining that to the merchant.”

Kree chuckles. “Deal.”

❖IN THE FOREST❖

As the caravan creaks through the quiet forest, three of the ashlings tumble off the wagon’s edge and scurry toward a patch of pale mushrooms glowing faintly in the underbrush.

Wilhelm notices first. “They’re wandering again,” he groans, already reaching for the barrel lid. “If they’d just stay put, we wouldn’t have this problem.”

Kree crouches beside one of the curious little creatures, its ember-like eyes reflecting his own. “They’re not clockwork, Wilhelm,” he says, exasperated. “They’re alive. Let them look.”

Wilhelm snorts. “They can look from inside the barrel. It’s not a museum out here—it’s a forest full of foxes and rain and—”

Kree folds his wings tight, feathers bristling. “You’d stuff the sun in a box if it made your journey easier.”

“That’s called common sense!” Wilhelm snaps, throwing up his hands. “We have forty of them! We can’t chase every glowing puff that—”

Thalasstheos’s voice cuts through the argument like a blade through mist. “Enough.”

The two freeze. The water genasi’s serene face has gone cold, her sea-colored eyes fixed on the path. “They are gone.”

The words hang heavy. Kree turns sharply, scanning the brush. The patch of mushrooms still glows—but the three small lights that had surrounded them are missing.

Wilhelm tightens his jaw. “They can’t have gone far. But—listen, Thalasstheos—if we waste time chasing every stray, we’ll lose the wagon, and Marta’s shipment, and possibly the road.”

Her gaze shifts to him, calm but unwavering, like deep water before a storm. “We do not abandon those who trust us.”

Wilhelm opens his mouth to argue—but the look in her eyes stills him.

Kree exhales and lowers his hood. “We promised,” he says quietly.

Thalasstheos nods once, the decision is made. “I will keep the wagon safe. Go. Bring them home.”

The forest seems to listen as Kree spreads his wings and slips into the undergrowth, Wilhelm following reluctantly with pistol drawn. Behind them, Thalasstheos kneels by the wagon, the remaining thirty-seven ashlings gathering close to her warmth as the mist thickens around the trees.

❖IN THE FOREST❖
— The other forest, off the trail —

The forest feels thicker the farther they go, the air damp with the smell of moss and smoke. Tiny flecks of ash dot the ground, glowing faintly like dying embers — the only sign of the missing ashlings.

Wilhelm trudges along the faint path, muttering under his breath. “I used to fix clockwork birds and teapots,” he grumbles, kicking a root. “Taught lectures about safe handling of alchemical reagents. Now I’m crawling through mud chasing living soot.”

Kree chuckles quietly, wings brushing past a fern. “You don’t sound thrilled.”

“I’m soaked, I’m bitten, and my boots cost more than this whole forest,” Wilhelm continues, voice rising. “And if one more magical creature needs rescuing, I might just retire early.”

Kree smirks, eyes scanning the ground. “You don’t strike me as the retiring type. You strike me as the complaining type.”

Wilhelm huffs but doesn’t answer. His eyes catch something — small handprints of ash on a root, faint squeaks up ahead. “There,” he says, gesturing forward. “Luck favors the miserable.”

They creep closer until they see a small clearing: a crooked tree, its roots half-exposed, and three ashlings clinging to the bark, trembling. Beneath the branches, a fox prowls, its eyes twin embers in the twilight.

The fox snarls. The ashlings squeak in terror, pressing closer to the trunk.

Wilhelm doesn’t hesitate. With a sharp click and a flash, his flintlock cracks the air. The fox drops mid-step, a curl of smoke rising from its fur.

Kree flinches at the sound. “Gods—was that necessary?”

Wilhelm reloads briskly, kneeling beside the creature. “It was hungry,” he says flatly. “Now it’s dinner. Waste not.” He tugs at the pelt with practiced hands, the gesture mechanical.

Kree’s feathers ruffle. “You could’ve scared it off.”

Wilhelm glances up, tired eyes meeting his. “You think everything can be scared off, don’t you? Some things only listen once.”

Kree looks away, silent. The ashlings, still trembling, peek from behind the bark and emit a few tentative chirps. Wilhelm sighs and gestures toward them. “Go on, birdman. Tell them the hero saved the day.”

Kree doesn’t answer. He gathers the three ashlings carefully in his cloak, their faint light flickering like candleflames against the dusk.

For a long while, neither speaks as they make their way back toward the caravan — the sound of Wilhelm’s boots and Kree’s feathers brushing the undergrowth filling the silence between them.

❖BACK AT THE WAGON❖

The sun hangs low in the sky, slanting through the trees in warm, dappled light as Wilhelm and Kree emerge from the forest path. The three missing ashlings cling to Kree’s cloak, faintly glowing with relief.

Thalasstheos looks up from where she sits beside the wagon, the other ashlings clustered near her hands. She smiles faintly at the sight of the returnees. “They are safe,” she says, her tone full of quiet gratitude. Then her gaze lingers on them — noticing the strain in Kree’s shoulders and the powder-smoke on Wilhelm’s sleeve.

“Were they frightened?” she asks softly, standing to meet them.

Kree shifts, feathers rustling. “We found them by a tree,” he says stiffly. “They’re fine now.”

Wilhelm scratches the back of his neck. “A fox,” he adds quickly. “Just a threat. Nothing serious.” The lie comes out smooth but shallow, and he avoids her eyes.

Thalasstheos studies him for a long heartbeat, the way one might study a current beneath the surface. Then she nods once. “Very well.”

Kree glances sideways at Wilhelm — a long, disappointed look that says everything words don’t. Wilhelm pretends not to notice, fiddling with his flintlock and forcing a grin.

“Well,” he mutters, “at least no one got eaten.”

The joke falters in the quiet. The ashlings huddle closer to Thalasstheos as she kneels to check the rescued ones for injuries, her voice calm and distant as she hums a soft melody that smells faintly of salt and rain.

The mood lingers — not broken, but strained — as they prepare the wagon to move again. Dust rises behind the wheels, and the forest falls quiet except for the soft crunch of boots and the faint, flickering chatter of the little fire spirits following close behind.

Chapter IV

Crossing the River

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

Later the party reaches a small but swift river—about twenty feet across. There is no bridge and the current looks dangerous, especially for the tiny ashlings.

Wilhelm squints at the river, its waters rushing faster than he expected. He sighs, adjusting his holster. “Do you… happen to have a spell for vanishing rivers?” he asks, glancing at Thalasstheos hopefully.

Thalasstheos tilts her head, eyebrows raised in mild confusion. “I… do not. Water I can shape, guide, or lift. But I cannot make an entire river disappear,” she says gently, her voice like calm tides.

Kree flaps a wing and leans over the edge of the bank, eyes gleaming with excitement. “Well, lucky for us, I have a better idea.”

Wilhelm groans. “Oh, I dread this.”

Kree crouches, spreading his wings. “We gather them in the barrel. I’ll lift it, fly them across. Easy as—well, flying.”

“Flying them across?” Wilhelm repeats, incredulous. “Do you know how heavy forty living things are? Do you?” He shakes his head, hands in the air. “We’ll topple, we’ll—oh, I can see it now—everyone drowned because of a bird and a barrel!”

Kree grins, scarred beak catching the sunlight. “Relax, human. I’m strong. They’re light. Trust me.”

Thalasstheos chuckles softly, shaking her head. “If you can carry them safely, do so—but remember: they are living beings, not cargo.”

Wilhelm mutters, exasperated, brushing a hand through his hair. “I didn’t sign up to be a river-crossing supervisor for glowing soot critters…”

Kree flaps his wings experimentally, the ashlings chittering nervously in the barrel. “You’ll survive. Maybe. Probably. Focus on looking heroic for once.”

Wilhelm scowls, but a reluctant smirk tugs at his lips. “Heroic… yes. That’s exactly what I’m here for.”

Kree cackles, hopping into position. “Then hold on, little ones! Adventure awaits!”

Thalasstheos murmurs a quiet blessing as Kree begins to lift the barrel, her laughter soft as water over rocks, blending with the ashlings’ excited squeaks.

The ashlings are unexpectedly light when gathered together; Kree lifts the barrel, flies, and lands on the far bank. There is a small disaster: a hole in the barrel—an old bullet hole from the bandit fight—causes several ashlings to fall into the water. Wilhelm leaps to help but is pulled by the current himself.

❖THE SECOND UH-OH MOMENT❖

Kree flaps his wings with precision, the barrel of thirty-seven ashlings wobbling in his grip. He hovers above the river, then arcs gracefully across, landing safely on the far bank. The ashlings tumble out, squeaking in excitement, their tiny limbs scattering as they scramble toward Thalasstheos.

Wilhelm watches, heart pounding, as a few ashlings teeter dangerously near the edge of the barrel. Without thinking twice, he leaps into the rushing current, flailing wildly. “Hold still, you little glowing nuisances! I will not let you drown!”

The river grabs him instantly, yanking him downstream with terrifying force. Splashing, sputtering, and half-cursing the entire concept of adventure, Wilhelm struggles to stay upright. “This… this is preposterous! Completely preposterous!”

Thalasstheos moves like a tide itself, calm and unhurried. She shapes the water into a massive, translucent bubble beneath Wilhelm and the ashlings, lifting them gently from the current. The ashlings squeak in delight, clinging to Wilhelm’s soaked jacket, while he sputters indignantly, dripping and trembling.

❖ON THE OTHER SIDE❖

Kree lands on the far bank, feathers glinting with sunlight and spray, and laughs. “Oh, Wilhelm… you look ridiculous! Like a drowned rat who insists on rescuing a pile of glowing muffins!”

Wilhelm glares through dripping hair. “I did save them! And they’re not muffins! This is… heroism!” He shakes a fist at Kree, water flying off his soaked sleeves.

Thalasstheos steps lightly across the river, water rippling beneath her feet as though it were solid ground, and guides the remaining ashlings safely to the bank. She gives Wilhelm a faint, approving smile. “You acted selflessly,” she says, voice calm as a quiet tide.

Wilhelm pants, soaked to the bone, brushing mud and water off his jacket. “Selfless, yes… but I could do without all this wet nonsense next time.”

Kree chuckles, plucking a small coin from his pocket and handing it to Bop, who squeaks in delighted thanks. “See? All in a day’s work. And you got to swim in style, Wilhelm.”

Wilhelm mutters something about rivers, mud, and ungrateful birds, but a small smile tugs at his lips. Thalasstheos’s presence is steady, Kree’s laughter infectious, and for a moment, the three of them — wet, grumpy, and exhausted — share a small, triumphant sense of camaraderie.

They leave the carriage behind for the moment and rest after the day’s tensions.

✦End of Session One✦

The bandits’ presence, the ashling community, and Thalasstheos’s arrival have turned what began as a simple courier job into a journey with unexpected responsibilities. The party now travels together—an uneasy trio bound by coin, compassion, and the small, strange things they have rescued.

Session One closed with the group camped near the river, the ashlings safe for now, and the road ahead toward Phandalin open but uncertain.

Chapter V

Session Summary: The Ravine, the Troll, and the Two Birds Up to No Good

✦ ═══════════ ❖ ═══════════ ✦

Morning comes bright and cool, the forest still damp from the night before. Wilhelm squelches with every step, his boots puffing out little bursts of water. Kree can barely walk straight from laughing, and even Thalasstheos gives a rare, tiny smirk at Wilhelm’s soggy misery.

That’s when something screams overhead.

A shadow plummets from the canopy, flailing, squawking, and then—thud—a kenku lands in a bush, bursts out of it, and throws her arms up as if this was all part of the plan.

“KENRA!” she chirps, mimicking three different triumphant voices at once.

Wilhelm blinks. Kree chirps back. Thalasstheos just… waits.

Kenra introduces herself in a patchwork of borrowed tones, half-druidic wisdom and half-chaotic nonsense. Somehow, it works. She decides instantly that she’s joining the group; the group decides instantly that they don’t really get a say in it.

They set off.

The rain begins as a fine mist, barely more than breath against the skin, but quickly thickens into a steady drizzle. Drops slide off the edges of Kree’s feathers as he hunches protectively over the barrel, wings wrapped around it like a brooding mother hen.

Inside, the ashlings buzz and squeak, little sparks flickering as they try to peek out.

The path slopes sharply downward, mud slick beneath their boots and claws. Trees open like a curtain—and the world suddenly drops away in front of them.

A vast ravine stretches out, carved deep into the earth like a wound. Fog clings to its sides, curling up in pale tendrils. A rope-and-plank bridge—old, moss-covered, swaying even without wind—connects their ledge to the opposite side. Far across it, squatting in the mud like a boulder that forgot to be a rock, sits a troll.

It gnaws on something vaguely limb-shaped.

Wilhelm stops dead. “Well. That’s… welcoming.”

Kenra tilts her head, eyes narrowing with mischief. “Maybe… guard distracted if big scary beast nearby.” She puffs up, inhales dramatically, and produces the most unconvincing “GRAAAARRR” a creature has ever made.

Wilhelm stares. “No. Absolutely not.”

Kenra makes the noise again, louder. “Grrr? RAWR?”

“You sound like a sick goat trying to fight a squirrel.”

She huffs, feathers ruffling. “Plan better than nothing.”

“We haven’t even discussed if we WANT to cross!” Wilhelm snaps. He gestures sharply at the ravine. “That bridge looks like it was built by drunk raccoons.”

Thalasstheos steps forward, rain sliding off her blue skin like she’s part of the weather itself. “We must cross. The Old House lies beyond. The ashlings cannot wait.”

Wilhelm sighs dramatically. “Of course. Why wouldn’t the ancient haunted ruin be on the other side of the death bridge guarded by a—”

A deep, wet voice booms from across the chasm:

“HEY! Travelers!”

The troll waves a large hand. “Fee! Twenty-five gold pieces! Each!”

Wilhelm’s eye twitches.

“You’re the newest member—”

Kree straightens indignantly. “No she isn’t! Kenra literally fell from sky this morning—”

Wilhelm holds up a hand. “Irrelevant.”

Thalasstheos tilts her head. “You are suggesting I… what? Pay? Distract? Be sacrificed?”

Wilhelm gestures helplessly. “I’m suggesting we use… resources efficiently.”

Her expression flattens. “I am not a resource.”

“Well—”

“Nor cargo. Nor trade.” Her voice sharpens, a cool undertone like a rising tide. “I joined you to protect the ashlings, not to be bartered like a goat.”

The drizzle thickens into rain.

Wilhelm rubs his temples. “I wasn’t bartering you, I was—look—fine. Fine. We’ll do it your way.”

Thalasstheos’s stare is cool enough to make him turn away first.

Kree steps between them quickly. “We go. We talk. No arguing on slippery cliff, please.”

Kenra nods vigorously. “Yes. Less talk, more… crossing without dying.”

With a collective, soggy sigh, the group approaches the bridge.

Thalasstheos takes the lead, grace steady even on the shifting planks. When they reach shouting distance, she raises a hand.

“Greetings,” she calls. “We seek safe passage across.”

The troll grins wide enough to show every mossy tooth. “Fee!”

Wilhelm mutters, “Oh, brilliant negotiation.”

Kenra decides this is the perfect moment to help. She straightens, puffs up her feathers… and emits what she thinks is a terrifying bear roar.

It is, instead, a high-pitched, startled “MROWWR-krk!” that echoes pitifully off the ravine walls.

The troll scratches his head. Then frowns.

✦The Five-Minute Party Huddle (aka: Wilhelm’s Descent into Madness)✦

The group pulls back from the bridge, huddling beneath a soggy pine.

Thalasstheos: “The fee is the fee.”

Kree: “We pay… but perhaps not permanently.”

Wilhelm: “Absolutely not. We’re not giving that overgrown moss-monster a mountain of gold.”

Kenra (brightly): “I roar again?”

“NO!”

Thalasstheos counts their coins calmly.

Wilhelm: “I have thirty.”

Kree: “Ten.”

Kenra: “Ten.”

There is silence.

Then Wilhelm: “…that’s ninety.”

Thalasstheos: “uh...”

Wilhelm rubs his temples. “We need one hundred.”

Kree raises a feathered finger. “We have one hundred.”

Wilhelm blinks. “No, we don’t.”

Kenra nods as if explaining something to a child. “We have ninety.”

Kree: “And ten.”

Wilhelm: “THAT’S STILL NINETY—THAT’S THE SAME NINETY!”

Thalasstheos, calmly: “We can remove someone from the equation.”

Wilhelm glares. “You mean me, don’t you.”

She smiles serenely. “You said it, not I.”

Kenra leans to Kree. “Is this math? Or arguing?”

Kree shrugs. “Both.”

Wilhelm throws his hands up. “Fine. We’ll pay. But mark my words—this troll is a walking scam.”

✦Paying the Toll✦

After five full minutes of nonsense, Wilhelm stomps forward and dumps the gold into the troll’s palm.

“There. HAPPY?”

“Yes,” the troll says, delighted. “Cross!”

They shuffle across the swaying bridge—Thalasstheos calm, Wilhelm muttering, Kenra proud of her earlier “roar,” and Kree pretending not to fiddle with the troll’s belt pouch.

✦The Barrel Problem✦

Halfway across, the troll suddenly squints.

“What in the box?”

Wilhelm answers so fast it squeaks: “Potatoes! Old! Dusty! Very boring. Terrible potatoes.”

Kree, meanwhile, has already managed to liberate the 100 gold from the troll’s belt—so smoothly that he’s humming.

Thalasstheos, bless her unbroken honesty, sighs.

“They are ashlings.”

The troll gasps with delight. “Then fee for them, too! Twenty-five gold each!”

Wilhelm’s jaw drops so far it nearly hits the bridge.

“ALL FORTY OF THEM?! Do you think we’re MADE of gol—”

Kree, smiling warmly, reaches into his pocket and hands the troll a pouch.

“Here you go! One hundred gold.”

It is, unmistakably, the exact same 100 gold.

The troll beams. “Thanks!”

Wilhelm sputters. “Wha—where—how—”

Kenra pats Kree’s shoulder proudly. “Birds good at money.”

✦The “Infinite Gold Loop” Scam Begins✦

The troll flips through the gold. “That pays for four ashlings. Now pay for others.”

Wilhelm goes pale. “THIRTY-SIX MORE?!”

He turns to Thalasstheos. “So… hear me out—”

“No,” she says calmly.

“We could offer the newest member—”

“I will freeze your boots to the ground.”

“But it’s LOGICAL—”

“No.”

While the two argue again, Kree casually slips the gold back out of the troll’s hand during a moment of distraction. Kenra plucks a stray coin and hands it to him like an assistant in a magic trick.

The troll holds out its empty hand. “Next payment!”

Kree drops the exact same pouch into it.

“Here you are!”

The troll counts the coins—again.

Wilhelm watches the absurd cycle, eyes widening.

Kenra nods. “Yes. Troll dumb.”

Even Thalasstheos is forced to blink. “This… seems improbable.”

Kree grins proudly. “Birds good at improbable.”

✦And So It Continues…✦

Every time Wilhelm distracts the troll with another bad argument about sacrificing Thalasstheos, Kree steals the gold back; Kenra assists; and Kree pays again.

Gold into troll hand.

The troll never notices.

After what feels like an eternity of idiocy, the troll finally claps its hands.

“All Ashlings paid for! You go now!”

Wilhelm staggers away whispering, “I think I’ve actually gone insane.”

Thalasstheos pats his shoulder. “No. Merely outmaneuvered by birds.”

Kenra puffs up proudly.

Kree bows. “It is a gift.”

Their plan might even have worked.

But Kree slips.

The gold scatters.

The troll’s eyes narrow.

And combat erupts.

✦The Fight✦

The troll scoops Kree up and flings him like a feathery projectile straight into Wilhelm. Both crash into the mud, groaning.

Kenra doesn’t hesitate—not even long enough for Wilhelm to finish yelling “WAIT, DON’T—”.

Feathers ripple into fur, joints pop, limbs surge outward. In a heartbeat she is eight feet of furious black bear, and with a roar that is somehow both terrifying and deeply, absurdly Kenra, she bellows:

❖“BEHOLD— THE CONSEQUENCES OF MY ACTIONS!”❖

Then she charges.

Primal Savagery crackles across her claws, turning them glassy and acid-slick. She launches herself at the troll with the enthusiasm of someone tackling an unsupervised buffet, sinks her fangs into its flank, and shakes.

The troll howls, windmilling its arms as if trying to remember whether it’s allowed to hit a bear that’s also a person.

Meanwhile, Thalasstheos bolts for the barrel of ashlings. She flings her arms wide, water swirling up from the river like a liquid ribbon. With Shape Water, she lifts the barrel in a rising swell—like a magical elevator that’s trying very hard to be professional about this—and gently sweeps the ashlings to the far cliff.

“Hold on,” she murmurs, “and please don’t slosh.”

She sprints back, dropping to her knees beside Wilhelm and Kree. Healing magic pulses from her hands—soft, warm, and smelling faintly of sea salt—knitting bruises and cuts as they gasp for breath.

Wilhelm staggers upright, dripping, battered, and furious in the very dignified way only a half-drowned gunslinger can be.

“Alright,” he growls, “round two.”

He levels his pistol, squints, fires…

…and completely misses the troll.

The bullet whizzes past Kenra’s ear.

Kenra freezes mid-maul, slowly turns her big bear head, and gives Wilhelm a look that can only be described as “Really?”

Wilhelm winces. “I was… giving it a warning shot.”

Kenra snorts. It sounds like a wet sneeze, but judgy.

Kree, now recovered and airborne, calls down, “Don’t worry, Wilhelm! I’ll hit it for you!”

He flips a dagger through his fingers, wings flaring dramatically, and throws it.

THUNK. It embeds right in the troll’s shoulder.

Kree beams. “See? Like that!”

Wilhelm mutters, “Show-off,” but he can’t help the proud little smile tugging at his mouth.

The troll roars, smashes its club into Kenra, then charges Wilhelm. It kicks him squarely in the chest—slamming him into a tree so hard it shakes loose a rain of leaves.

Thalasstheos sprints toward the fray, water whipping around her ankles as if urging her forward. She barely gets her hands up before—

❖WHAM.❖

The troll’s massive arm swings out blindly, its knotted knuckles smashing into her ribs with the force of a battering ram. The impact lifts her clean off the ground, her breath leaving in a sharp, pained gasp.

But she doesn’t hit the dirt.

Kree dives beneath her, wings beating frantically. For a moment he almost crumples under the weight, wobbling like a bird trying to land with an entire library in its arms—but he manages, trembling, to lower her gently to the earth.

“Don’t—move,” he wheezes.

Thalasstheos tries to speak, winces, and instead clenches her jaw. Pain arcs across her features, sharp and bright—but her eyes stay focused.

She presses her palms together.

The river answers.

A deep, crystalline crack echoes out as water surges upward and freezes mid-flow, exploding into a tall, jagged ice wall. Its surface gleams like fractured sapphire, separating the troll from her fallen companions with a barrier as beautiful as it is deadly.

Only then does she turn to Wilhelm, kneeling beside him with the tender precision of someone who’s done this a thousand times. Her thumbs rest lightly against his temples, guiding his breath into steadier rhythm, her magic knitting tension from his chest.

On the other side of the ice wall, Kenra roars.

This time it isn’t a joke or a battle quip—it’s a full-throated, primal bear roar, shaking the air like rolling thunder. She slams into the troll with raw, wild fury, claws dripping with acid and teeth bared in unwavering rage.

Snow-like frost, kicked up by Thalasstheos’s magic, settles on Kenra’s dark fur as she rips into the monster.

Thalasstheos rises unsteadily, raises one hand—

—and a swirl of glittering ice shards coalesces around her, a storm of tiny knives catching the moonlight. She flings them forward, and they streak across the battlefield, slicing through the air with a haunting, crystalline whisper.

They pepper the troll’s hide, freezing, melting, and freezing again as they bite deep.

Wilhelm wipes blood from his lip, pushes to his feet, and charges—gun empty, fists clenched, eyes locked on the beast. Determination radiates off him in waves.

On his right, Kree swoops in low, wings cutting through the air, a second dagger glinting between his claws.

They hit the troll from opposite sides in a perfect pincer: Wilhelm slamming into its lower spine with a shoulder-check that rattles teeth, Kree carving a bright, shallow arc along its exposed flank.

The troll spins, confused, enraged—

—and Kenra clamps her jaws down on its arm, anchoring it with unstoppable force.

Her teeth sink deep. The troll bellows, stumbling, pinned in place as Wilhelm and Kree ready the finishing strikes.

The session freezes there—

Waiting for a victory they can almost taste.

Session 3, Bridge to The Manor.

With Shape Water, she lifts the barrel in a rising swell—like a magical elevator that’s trying very hard to be professional about this—and gently sweeps the ashlings to the far side of the bridge. They squeak in a chorus of alarm and excitement as they bob along inside the rolling current, tiny lights flickering like lanterns in a storm.

“Safe,” Thalasstheos murmurs, guiding the water down and setting the barrel gently against the rock. The ashlings huddle together, chirping anxiously but unharmed.

Behind her, the ravine explodes into noise.

Kenra-the-bear slams into the troll again, claws scraping against rubbery hide as acidic magic sizzles and smokes. The troll howls, staggering backward, one foot slipping dangerously close to the edge of the ravine.

“HEY!” it bellows, swatting wildly. “NO FAIR! YOU DIDN’T PAY FOR BEAR!”

Wilhelm groans from the mud, rolling onto his side as Kree groans on top of him.

“Get—off—me,” Wilhelm wheezes.

Kree scrambles upright, feathers slick with rain and muck. “You broke my dramatic landing,” he snaps, then looks up just in time to see the troll rearing back to smash Kenra with both fists.

“Oh. That’s bad.”

Wilhelm doesn’t argue. He yanks himself to his knees, hands shaking as he reloads his flintlock. Powder. Ramrod. Ball. His fingers fumble, slick with river water and panic.

“I swear,” he mutters, teeth clenched, “if this misfires—”

The troll brings its fists down.

Kenra barely dodges, rolling aside with a thunderous whump that rattles the bridge. Planks snap. Ropes creak.

Wilhelm’s shot rang out sharp and final, the bullet striking the troll square in the face with a wet crack. The creature reeled, roaring in pain, just as Kenra—still fully bear—thundered into it. Her claws raked deep through rubbery flesh, her weight driving the troll backward toward the edge of the bridge. For a heartbeat, it seemed like brute force and precision would be enough.

Then the troll howled.

Its rage came all at once. It tore itself free from Kenra’s grasp and charged Wilhelm with terrifying speed. The club came down once, then again—wood and bone colliding—and Wilhelm was sent crashing to the planks. The bridge shuddered beneath him as the troll beat him into stillness, leaving him crumpled and unmoving.

“Wilhelm!”

Thalasstheos sprinted forward, skidding to her knees beside him as silver moonlight bloomed in her hands. She raised her gaze just long enough to fix the troll in her sight and called down a radiant beam from above. Moonlight burned into the creature’s flesh as Kree darted in, wings snapping as he lunged with his dagger, slashing where the light weakened it. The troll staggered, howled one last time, and collapsed—its body dissolving into silence and steam.

For a moment, only the wind and the creaking bridge remained.

Thalasstheos immediately turned back to Wilhelm, pressing glowing hands to his chest, steady and focused. The magic flowed like a tide pulling him back from the brink. His breathing evened, shallow but present. She stayed there until she was sure—absolutely sure—that he would live.

Once the danger had passed, they crossed the rest of the derelict bridge carefully. On the far side, half-hidden beneath damp moss and splintered boards, sat a sturdy chest. Kree’s eyes lit up instantly.

“Ooooh—shiny box, shiny box,” he crooned, already kneeling beside it. He pulled out his thieves’ tools, humming to himself in a bright, sing-song rhythm as metal clicked softly. “Open-open-open…”

The lock gave way easily. Inside lay gold coins, neatly packed gear, and well-worn equipment—helmets, belts, and blades clearly left behind by adventurers who had paid the toll to cross and never made it back. Kree rifled through it with obvious delight, holding things up to the light.

“Pretty pay. Bridge pay. Thank you, ghosts,” he chirped happily.

After dividing what they could carry, Kree flew back across the ravine, carefully lifting the barrel of ashlings and ferrying it over with surprising gentleness. Once reunited, Thalasstheos stabilized Wilhelm fully, and—after a short rest and many sore muscles—they pressed on.

The smell of ash grew stronger as they approached the mansion.

It loomed out of the trees like a forgotten thought, its stone walls swallowed by thick vines and soft green moss. Windows stared darkly from beneath creeping ivy, and the air around it felt warm in the wrong places, as if embers still smoldered somewhere deep inside. The ground was littered with fallen leaves and blackened stone.

At the entrance, they stopped.

“The ashlings should stay here,” Wilhelm said, glancing toward the flickering little creatures. “We don’t know what’s inside.”

Thalasstheos nodded. “I’ll guard them. That’s my duty.”

Reluctantly, the others agreed. Kree, Wilhelm, and Kenra moved into the courtyard while Thalasstheos stayed behind with the barrel, eyes never leaving the manor doors.

The courtyard opened around a cracked stone fountain, its basin dry and choked with leaves. Almost immediately, small fire spirits leapt down from the surrounding walls, sensing intrusion. Wilhelm reacted first, driving his spear cleanly through one—it dissipated instantly in a hiss of embers. Kree lunged at another with his dagger, but the spirit shrieked and surged forward, exploding in a burst of flame.

Wilhelm yelped as fire caught his coat.

“Ah—no—no no no—!”

He spun in circles, slapping at himself uselessly as smoke curled up around his shoulders.

From the entrance, Thalasstheos heard the commotion and rushed in, summoning a sheet of water that drenched him head to toe. Steam hissed as the flames died.

“You’re fine,” she said flatly.

“I was on fire,” Wilhelm replied, dripping.

They exchanged a look, then silently agreed it was safer for her to come along.

Inside the mansion, the air was stale and heavy. The party moved carefully, still worn from the bridge. Wilhelm scoffed at their caution.

“It’s a normal dungeon,” he muttered, strolling ahead.

The tile beneath his boot clicked.

He vanished straight down into a pit with a startled shout.

A few seconds later, Wilhelm climbed out of the pit, dust and grime covering him, muttering under his breath as he brushed himself off. A brief laugh ran through the group at the sight of him, but it was quickly cut off as a fire spirit burst from a nearby corridor, shrieking and spinning. Kenra’s laughter, which had been bright and carefree, froze mid-chuckle as the spirit exploded in her face.

The impact sent her staggering backward, eyes wide and feathers bristling with fear. Her claws scrabbled against the cold stone floor as she bolted down a narrow hallway, heart racing, leaving a small trail of scorched debris behind her.

The room she stumbled into was dark and quiet, abandoned long ago. Broken shelves lined the walls, their contents either rotted away or coated in dust. A long table dominated the center, and upon it rested a small potion in a cracked vial and a faintly glowing torch, still magically alight. Kenra skidded to a stop, panting, and eyed the objects cautiously.

She crouched, sniffing the potion and tapping the torch. A flicker of curiosity sparked in her fear. “Huh… shiny… shiny safe…” she murmured, picking up the torch and holding it out to light the dim corners.

Meanwhile, back in the hall, Thallasstheos called out.

“Kenra! Kree! Stay close!”

Her voice echoed off the empty walls. Wilhelm, still recovering from the pit, waved weakly.

“Kree! Where are you?” she added, frustration creeping into her tone.

But Kree’s head was tilted down a side corridor, wings half-folded, eyes fixed on a faint, pulsing red glow ahead. The glow hypnotized him, and with a soft whistle, he moved toward it, wings lifting as he disappeared down the hallway.

The nursery lay in quiet disarray. Dusty toys littered the floor—wooden blocks, threadbare dolls, miniature furniture, and tiny rattles. In the center, a small table held a velvet-lined box. Its contents pulsed with an inner light: a red amulet, glowing warmly in the dimness.

Kree’s eyes widened.

“Oh… pretty…,” he cooed softly. Delicate claws carefully lifted the amulet and tucked it into his cloak, brushing the fabric over it protectively. He crouched there for a moment, listening to the faint echo of footsteps in the hall, before finally deciding to move back toward the others.

Thallasstheos’s voice rose again from the stairwell.

“Kree! Kenra! Where are you both?”

She sighed, exasperation clear in the tone. Seeing Wilhelm still half-stuck in the pit, she rolled up her sleeves and moved to pull him out herself, muttering under her breath.

Once Wilhelm was free, dusted off, and muttering about “never trusting dungeons again,” they pressed on. The bedroom at the end of the hallway was shadowed and quiet, the faint smell of old wood and candle wax lingering. Searching for the missing members, Wilhelm’s eyes landed on a silver candelabra atop a dresser. Without hesitation, he grabbed it, weighing it in his hands.

Thallasstheos’s eyes narrowed. “Wilhelm! We need to focus on finding the others, not taking trinkets!”

“They’re just small treasures!” Wilhelm protested, brushing off her scowl.

“This is not the time!” she snapped. “Kenra could be in danger, and Kree… who knows where that bird is?”

He grinned sheepishly, holding the candelabra closer. “It’s shiny… and sturdy. Perfect for lighting our path.”

Thallasstheos pinched the bridge of her nose. “Fine. Take it. But after we find the others. Priority: survival, not loot.”

Wilhelm nodded, tucking the candelabra into his pack.

A few moments later, all four of them converged at the foot of the stairwell, the dim light casting long shadows across the walls. Kenra emerged from the kitchen hallway, torch in paw, eyes wide but still flicking nervously toward the hall. Kree arrived a heartbeat later, wings fluttering softly, the red amulet safely concealed within his cloak.

Thallasstheos exhaled deeply, taking in the scattered, weary party. “Alright,” she muttered. “Let’s move. And please… no more detours, or I swear I’ll carry all of you myself.”

The stairwell was narrow and worn, the stones slick with moss and dust from years of neglect. Each step echoed with their boots and claws, Wilhelm grumbling quietly about his pitfall experience while Kenra padded silently beside him, still clutching her torch tightly. Thallasstheos brought up the rear, arms crossed, her eyes narrowed as she scowled at the two.

“Wilhelm,” she said, voice low and steady, “and Kenra… do you really think it’s acceptable to take from the Ashlings’ home?”

Wilhelm shifted uneasily. “It’s just… a candelabra. They’ll never miss it.”

Kenra cawed, “And I only took the potion to keep myself alive! Technically… nothing wrong there.”

Thallasstheos huffed, exasperated. “You don’t get it! Their home is sacred. We are guests, not raiders.”

Kree, tucked just behind the railing, shifted uncomfortably, the red amulet snugly hidden beneath his cloak. He cooed softly to himself, shame creeping into his movements, and kept his wings folded tightly, hoping no one would notice.

Once they reached the outer wall, the view opened dramatically. The manor sprawled below them, vines clinging to broken stone walls, moss softening the edges of collapsed towers. Beyond, the forest stretched like a rolling green sea, sunlight glinting off dew on the leaves, the wind carrying the faint scent of ash from their earlier travels. Birds flitted between the branches, and distant mountains framed the horizon.

“It’s… breathtaking,” Wilhelm muttered, momentarily forgetting the tension.

Kenra let out a low chuckle. “Even for a haunted old place… wow.”

The beauty was fleeting, however, as a corner of the wall suddenly erupted. A crumbling fireplace, long dormant, spouted fire spirits that hissed and spiraled toward them, licking the stone edges with heat and fury.

Kree reacted instinctively. He leapt into the air, wings slicing the wind, talons gripping his shortbow. “Incoming! Fizzle-fizzle, pow!” he squawked, releasing arrows at two of the spirits. Both dissipated in harmless sparks, leaving a faint trail of smoke.

But more spirits poured from the fireplace, swirling around with menacing intent. Thallasstheos’s hands rose, water magic forming in a glowing arc above the flames. Streams of shimmering liquid lanced out, hissing as they extinguished one spirit after another.

Yet one spirit resisted. Flickering violently, it surged upward, cracking and sparking as it coalesced into a wall of fire. Kree, trapped against the stone, found himself encased in a shimmering bubble of flames. The tongues of fire licked at his feathers, singeing the edges of his wings, and heat pressed in from all sides.

Kenra was crushed beneath the blow.

Her wolf form shattered, collapsing back into her kenku shape as she lay motionless.

It turned again.

Thalasstheos barely had time to look up before its jaws closed around her.

It shook its head once—

And flung her across the chamber.

She hit the wall hard and dropped, unmoving.

Kree rose again into the air, fury burning through him.

He drew arrow after arrow, firing in rapid succession. Each shot struck true, embedding into molten flesh.

The salamander roared—

Then slid back into a pool of lava.

Its wounds began to close.

Healing.

“No—!” Kree shouted.

The creature surged out again, renewed.

It barreled forward—

Striking Wilhelm and Kenra before they could react.

Both fell.

Still.

Silent.

For a moment—

There was nothing.

Then—

A faint glow.

Thalasstheos.

Her eyes opened slowly as she dragged herself upright, trembling. Her body screamed in protest, but she raised her hands anyway.

This was it.

Everything she had left.

Light poured from her—not fire, not heat, but something softer. Ancient. Steady.

It spread across the battlefield.

Over Wilhelm.

Over Kenra.

Over Kree.

Wounds closed. Breath returned.

Strength—just enough.

Then the light faded.

Thalasstheos nearly collapsed again.

Kree didn’t wait.

With a sharp beat of his wings, he launched forward, sword blazing in his grip.

Wilhelm rolled to his feet, raising his gun with one final, steady aim.

Kenra, barely conscious, pushed herself up and hurled a dagger with everything she had left.

Thalasstheos lifted her hand one last time.

Moonlight broke through the fire.

A beam of pale radiance descended from above, cutting through the inferno and pinning the salamander in place.

All at once—

They struck.

The sword drove deep.

The bullet fired true.

The dagger found its mark.

The moonbeam burned.

The salamander let out a final, shuddering roar.

Its body cracked.

Split.

Collapsed inward.

The fire dimmed—

Faltered—

And went out.

Silence fell.

The chamber, once blazing with life and fury, now lay still.

Only cooling stone remained.

Kree hovered for a moment longer before landing unsteadily.

Wilhelm lowered his weapon.

Kenra didn’t move.

Thalasstheos exhaled slowly, her strength finally giving way as she sank to her knees.

It was over.

The fire was gone.

And somehow—

They were still standing.

They didn’t leave the chamber so much as crawl out of it.

The heat was fading, but the structure wasn’t holding. Cracks split through the stone, molten seams cooling too fast, too unevenly. Pieces of the ceiling gave way as they staggered through the doorway—half carrying, half dragging one another.

Kree stumbled more than once, still clutching the black sword.

Wilhelm leaned heavily on what remained of his strength, one arm braced against the wall, the other gripping his weapon out of sheer habit.

Kenra moved last, unsteady but determined, feathers singed and dampened by sweat and soot.

Thalasstheos nearly collapsed at the threshold.

But they made it out.

Cool air hit them like a blessing.

They didn’t go far—just beyond the ruins, where the ground turned soft and the grass bent gently under a growing drizzle.

Rain began to fall.

Light. Steady.

Enough to quiet the last traces of heat clinging to their skin.

They sank down where they stood.

No watch.

No words.

Just rest.

When they woke, the world felt… different.

The fire was gone.

Not hidden. Not dormant.

Gone.

The land itself seemed to breathe easier.

Shapes began to gather at the edge of the clearing.

The ashlings.

No longer frantic or flickering wildly, but calm—small, ember-soft figures that moved like fading coals rather than raging flame.

They approached slowly.

Carefully.

Gratefully.

One stepped forward—Bop.

Wilhelm recognized them immediately.

Bop carried something folded over their arms.

Without ceremony, they held it out.

A coat.

Simple—but new. Untouched by flame.

Wilhelm stared at it for a moment before taking it, running his hand over the fabric like he didn’t quite believe it.

“…Thank you,” he said quietly.

And for once, there was no complaint in his voice.

Another ashling approached Kenra.

They reached up, placing something gently into her hands—

A feather.

Dark, but faintly warm, like it still held a memory of fire.

Kenra turned it over slowly, eyes reflecting its glow.

Thalasstheos was next.

An ashling placed a small, luminous pearl in her palm. It shimmered faintly, like moonlight caught beneath the surface.

She bowed her head in thanks, her expression soft.

Kree didn’t notice his gift at first.

He was too busy watching the others.

Until something clinked against the ground near his feet.

He looked down.

And froze.

A coin.

Platinum.

Perfectly polished.

He picked it up slowly, holding it to the light, eyes wide.

“…This is,” he whispered, “an absurd amount of money.”

He looked at the ashlings.

Then back at the coin.

“…I accept.”

The ashlings lingered only a moment longer.

Then, like embers finally cooling, they drifted back into the land.

And were gone.

For a while, the party just stood there.

Breathing.

Letting the quiet settle in.

Eventually, Wilhelm adjusted his new coat.

Kree tucked the coin away—carefully.

Thalasstheos closed her hand around the pearl.

Kenra held onto the feather.

They turned.

And made their way back to the cart.

The road stretched ahead of them, damp from the rain, winding forward through a world that no longer burned.

Phandalin waited.

And this time—

They were finally on their way.
